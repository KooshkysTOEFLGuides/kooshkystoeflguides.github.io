Beyond “More Than”

A practical guide to the full comparative system—from taller than and more useful than to classification, ellipsis, rather than, other than, and precise TOEFL-ready comparisons.

Chapter index

    Chapter 01

    The big idea behind comparative structures

    Most learners first meet comparisons through sentences such as Mina is taller than Sara or This method is more effective than the old one. Those are only the basic shapes. A comparison can contain a long description, a quantity, two competing labels, a complete idea, an omitted verb, or a special expression whose meaning is no longer a simple comparison.

    Key point

    A comparative sentence normally contains two sides:

    the stronger / weaker / larger / smaller side + than + the comparison point

    The words before than tell us what difference exists. The words after than identify the comparison point: a person, thing, action, expectation, time, place, number, or complete idea.

    This guide uses a few short grammar labels. When a highlighted term such as modifier, complement, or ellipsis appears, you can click it to open its plain-English explanation in the grammar toolbox.

    Chapter 02

    A very quick review of the familiar patterns

    This chapter is a reminder, not a full lesson. Before the ordinary patterns, notice the most common irregular forms: they appear throughout this guide and do not follow the usual -er or more rules.

    Irregulars to know first

    Good describes a person or thing, while well usually describes how an action is performed; however, both become better in a comparison. In the same way, bad and badly both become worse.

    good / well → better

    The revised plan is better than the original. She explained the idea better than I did.

    bad / badly → worse

    The second error was worse than the first. The machine performed worse than expected.

    many / much → more

    The course includes more examples than before and requires more time than the shorter version.

    little → less; few → fewer

    The new task requires less preparation and contains fewer questions.

    far → farther / further

    The new campus is farther away; the abstract consequences may extend further than expected.

    Short comparative adjective + than

    Many short adjectives take -er.

    • The second room is larger than the first.
    • Public transport is cheaper than driving.
    • The task was easier than I expected.

    more / less + adjective or adverb + than

    Longer descriptions normally use more or less.

    • The new rule is more practical than the old one.
    • She answered more confidently than before.
    • The process is less expensive than expected.

    more / fewer / less + noun + than

    Use these to compare numbers and amounts.

    • We interviewed more students than last year.
    • The second essay contained fewer errors than the first.
    • The new system uses less energy than the old one.

    Comparative adverb + than

    The comparison can describe how an action is performed.

    • Nadia spoke more confidently than Amir.
    • The second group finished faster than the first.
    • She handled the question better than she had during practice.
    Remember

    Do not use two comparative markers together: better, not more better; easier, not more easier; worse, not more worse.

    Chapter 03

    Choose the correct comparative form

    Use -er with many short adjectives

    Most one-syllable adjectives use -er. The spelling may change, but the meaning does not.

    Examples
    • small → smaller: The online class was smaller than the in-person class.
    • large → larger: The second sample was larger than the first.
    • easy → easier: The revised instructions are easier to follow than the original ones.
    • big → bigger: The long-term problem may be bigger than the immediate one.

    Use more with long or complete descriptions

    Most adjectives with three or more syllables use more. Many two-syllable adjectives also prefer it, especially in formal writing.

    Examples
    • The second approach is more reliable than the first.
    • This explanation is more intellectually demanding than it initially appears.
    • The policy became more politically difficult to defend than its supporters had anticipated.
    • Her final response was more carefully organized than her first attempt.

    Two useful irregular distinctions

    farther / further

    Farther is especially common for physical distance: The station is farther than I thought. Further is common for additional or abstract extension: We need further discussion. In many distance comparisons, both are accepted.

    older / elder

    Use older than in ordinary comparisons: Mina is older than Reza. Elder is mainly used before a family noun—my elder sister—and normally not with than.

    Some two-syllable adjectives allow both forms

    Words such as simple, quiet, clever, narrow, and gentle may appear with either pattern. One form may sound more natural in a particular sentence.

    simpler than

    Short, direct, and usually more natural: The revised method is simpler than the original.

    more simple than

    Possible when directly contrasting two qualities: The plan is more simple than elegant. Here, the meaning is closer to “simple rather than elegant.”

    Use less to compare a lower degree

    Less works before both adjectives and adverbs, including short ones, although an opposite -er form may sometimes sound more natural.

    Examples
    • The new route is less direct than the old one.
    • Students responded less confidently than they had during practice.
    • The second solution is less costly than the first.
    • Less easy than is grammatical, but harder than is usually more natural.
    Chapter 04

    Compare complete descriptions—not only single words

    The element before than can be an entire description. Keep all the words that naturally belong to that description inside the comparison.

    Keep an adjective’s natural partner with it

    more / less + adjective + its natural partner + than ...
    Examples
    • Students are more concerned about housing costs than about tuition fees.
    • The new policy is less harmful to small businesses than the earlier proposal.
    • The second explanation is more relevant to first-year students than to experienced researchers.
    • This method is more suitable for short-term analysis than for long-term prediction.

    Compare actions with to + verb

    Examples
    • The software is easier to install than to maintain.
    • It is more useful to explain one idea fully than to list five undeveloped ideas.
    • Students may find the concept harder to apply than to understand.
    • The committee was more likely to delay the decision than to reject the proposal completely.

    The second to can sometimes disappear—easier to install than maintain—but keeping it often makes a long sentence clearer and more balanced.

    Compare two combined qualities or manners

    Examples
    • Her answer was more direct and better supported than her previous one.
    • The new system operates more quietly and efficiently than the old machinery.
    • The problem is less financial and more administrative than the report suggests.
    • The response should be more carefully reasoned than aggressively worded.

    Use the ordinary noun order—and recognize the formal reversed form

    Normal and strongly preferred

    It was a more serious problem than anyone had realized.

    Formal and less common

    It was more serious a problem than anyone had realized.

    The reversed form is grammatical, but it is much less common than as serious a problem as. For normal TOEFL writing, use a more serious problem than unless you have a clear stylistic reason not to.

    Chapter 05

    Compare numbers, amounts, measurements, and exact differences

    More, fewer, and less

    more

    Works with plural countable nouns and uncountable nouns: more students, more evidence, more time.

    fewer

    Use with plural countable nouns: fewer students, fewer mistakes, fewer meetings.

    less

    Use with uncountable nouns and measurements: less information, less traffic, less than two hours.

    Examples
    • The second group produced more ideas than the first but provided less evidence than the professor expected.
    • The revised survey contains fewer questions than the original and therefore requires less time than before.
    • The city invested more money in public transport than in road expansion.
    • The new course gives students more opportunities to speak than the previous course did.

    Less than with measurements and totals

    Even when a measurement contains a plural-looking unit, English normally treats the whole measurement as one amount.

    Examples
    • The journey takes less than three hours.
    • The device weighs less than ten kilograms.
    • The repair cost less than fifty euros.
    • The difference was less than five percentage points.

    More than one usually takes a singular verb

    Common difficulty

    Although the meaning is plural, the standard pattern is More than one student has complained, not have complained. With more than two students, use a plural verb normally.

    State the exact difference

    Examples
    • The new figure is 20 percent higher than last year’s.
    • Attendance was 300 students higher than expected.
    • The second essay was two points better than the first.
    • Mina is three years older than her brother.
    • The final cost was €2,000 lower than the original estimate.

    You can also place the difference after the verb: The new figure exceeded last year’s by 20 percent.

    Be careful with exact multiples

    Precision note

    Three times more than is often interpreted inconsistently. For exact academic meaning, prefer three times as much as, three times as many as, or three times the amount of. Use 300% more than only when you genuinely mean four times the original total.

    Chapter 06

    Use comparison to classify, correct, and choose the better label

    More X than Y: X is the stronger or more accurate description

    The two sides do not have to be measurable qualities. They may be two labels, reactions, explanations, causes, or activities.

    Examples
    • She was more disappointed than angry.
    • The proposal is more ambitious than practical.
    • The course tests more analytical ability than factual memory.
    • The disagreement was more about values than about evidence.
    • The policy failed more because of poor implementation than because of its original design.

    Less X than Y: Y is the more accurate description

    Less X than Y does more than say that X exists at a lower degree. It often means that Y is the more accurate label: the response may contain some errors, but incompleteness explains it better. Keep the two descriptions parallel.

    Examples
    • The response was less incorrect than incomplete.
    • The obstacle is less technical than political.
    • The change was less a solution than a temporary delay.
    • His comments were less an explanation than an attempt to avoid responsibility.

    More of a noun than ...: the ordinary, flexible pattern

    Use more of a/an + noun + than when deciding which noun label fits better. This is the safest and most common version.

    Examples
    • The meeting became more of a political performance than a serious discussion.
    • His response was more of an excuse than an explanation.
    • The assignment was more of a challenge than the instructor had intended.
    • The delay created more of a problem for commuters than for local businesses.

    More a noun than ...: possible, but more compressed

    The of can sometimes disappear, especially when two clear category labels are contrasted. The result often sounds more formal, rhetorical, or literary.

    Common and conversational

    The book is more of a meditation than a history.

    Compressed and formal

    The book is more a meditation than a history.

    Dropping of is not equally natural with every noun. More of a nuisance than a danger usually sounds better than more a nuisance than a danger.

    Less of a noun than ...

    Less of a/an + noun + than measures how weakly a noun label applies. It is useful when something seemed likely to be a burden, surprise, threat, or problem but proved milder or different.

    Examples
    • The change was less of a surprise than the media suggested.
    • The course proved less of a burden than students had feared.
    • The error is less of a grammar problem than a problem of unclear reasoning.

    Not so much X as Y: directly correct the description

    Not so much X as Y means that X is not the best description and Y is more accurate, more important, or more responsible. X may still be partly true; the structure redirects the listener’s attention from X to Y. The two sides should have matching shapes: adjective with adjective, noun with noun, about-phrase with about-phrase, or reason with reason.

    not so much + the weaker description + as + the better description
    Examples and meanings
    • The problem is not so much financial as administrative.Money is involved, but poor administration is the main problem.
    • She was not so much angry as disappointed.She may have felt some anger, but disappointment describes her feelings better.
    • The task tests not so much memorization as the ability to organize ideas quickly.Memory may help, but fast organization is the skill that matters most.
    • The reform failed not so much because people opposed its goals as because they distrusted its implementation.The goals were not the main cause of failure; distrust about how the reform would be carried out was.
    • The debate is not so much about what machines can do as about what humans should allow them to do.Technical ability is part of the debate, but human responsibility is its central issue.
    • His answer was not so much inaccurate as irrelevant.The information may have been factually correct, but it did not answer the question.
    • The delay resulted not so much from a lack of effort as from an unrealistic schedule.People worked hard; the timetable itself created the delay.
    • The new rule does not so much remove choice as change where that choice is exercised.Choice still exists, but it now appears at a different stage. With verbs, English often uses does not so much X as Y.
    Usage note

    This pattern is useful when correcting an oversimplified claim without saying it is completely false. It is common in careful discussion and academic argument. Do not use it merely to compare two measurable degrees; use an ordinary comparative for that: The first result was more accurate than the second.

    Not so X as Y: the shorter form without much

    With two short descriptions—especially adjectives or participles—much can be omitted: not so angry as disappointed. The meaning is usually the same corrective meaning: Y is the better description. This form is grammatical, but it is less common in present-day everyday English and often sounds formal, literary, or deliberately compressed. For clear TOEFL writing, not so much X as Y is normally the safer and more flexible choice.

    Examples and meanings
    • She was not so angry as disappointed.Disappointment, rather than anger, was her main feeling.
    • The answer was not so inaccurate as irrelevant.Its central weakness was failure to address the question, not factual error.
    • The plan is not so impractical as incomplete.The plan might work, but important parts have not yet been developed.
    • The room was not so small as badly designed.Its size was manageable; poor use of the available space was the real problem.
    Do not overextend it

    The shorter form works best with brief, balanced descriptions. With nouns, long phrases, causes, or complete ideas, keep much: not so much a financial problem as an administrative one; not so much because X as because Y.

    Chapter 07

    Control the size and certainty of the difference

    Show a large difference

    much / farmuch better; far less expensive
    a lota lot more useful; a lot fewer mistakes
    considerably / substantiallyconsiderably higher; substantially less reliable
    significantly / dramaticallysignificantly more effective; dramatically lower
    Examples
    • The revised system is far more reliable than the original.
    • Students made considerably fewer errors than they had during the first week.
    • The long-term cost may be substantially higher than the initial price suggests.
    • The second treatment was significantly less effective than the first.

    Show a small difference

    a little / slightlya little clearer; slightly more formal
    somewhatsomewhat less convincing
    marginallymarginally higher; marginally fewer
    not muchnot much better; not much more expensive

    Even, still, and any

    Examples
    • The second explanation is even clearer than the first.
    • After the revision, the task became still more difficult than before.
    • Is the new version any better than the old one?
    • The additional examples did not make the argument any more convincing than it already was.

    Name the comparison limit precisely

    Examples
    • The response was longer than necessary.
    • The results were better than expected.
    • The problem is more serious than it first appeared.
    • Reliable evidence is more important than ever.
    • The process took longer than usual.
    • The new policy created more difficulties than anyone had predicted.
    Chapter 08

    What can come after than?

    The part after than identifies the comparison point. It can be short or long, but it must make clear what the first side is being compared with.

    A noun or pronoun

    A noun phrase can name the person or thing used as the standard. With a pronoun, everyday English often uses the object form—than me—while a complete form such as than I am is more explicit and can prevent ambiguity.

    Examples
    • Mina is taller than her brother.
    • The new route is safer than the old one.
    • She is more experienced than me.
    • She is more experienced than I am.
    Meaning can change

    She likes the professor more than me can mean “more than she likes me” or, for some readers, “more than I like the professor.” A full ending removes the doubt: more than she likes me / more than I do.

    A matched action phrase

    The second side can be an action beginning with to or -ing. Match the form on both sides so the reader can see the alternatives immediately. The second to is sometimes omitted, but it is worth keeping in long sentences.

    Examples
    • It is easier to describe the problem than to solve it.
    • Students learn more by revising their own work than by memorizing a model.
    • For a short response, it is more useful to develop one reason fully than to list several reasons briefly.

    A complete idea

    A full subject-and-verb idea is useful when the comparison involves an expectation, prediction, belief, or different person’s action. Include as much of the idea as the reader needs; a long ending is not a problem when it prevents confusion.

    Examples
    • The policy was more controversial than its supporters had expected it to be.
    • Students understood the lecture better than the professor thought they would.
    • The project required more time than we had originally budgeted for.
    • The evidence is less conclusive than the article suggests.

    A helper verb instead of a repeated action

    A helper verb carries the tense or possibility of an action that the reader can recover. Use do/does/did for an ordinary repeated verb; repeat be, have, or a modal such as can, will, or would when that is the helper already needed.

    Examples
    • She works harder than I do. Do replaces work.
    • The second group has improved more than the first has. Has replaces has improved.
    • The policy may cost more than experts said it would. Would replaces would cost.
    • Students can develop ideas more effectively than they think they can. Can replaces can develop ideas.

    A reduced academic ending

    Academic English often shortens a predictable passive idea: than was expected becomes than expected, and than had originally been planned becomes than originally planned. Use this only when it is obvious what was expected, planned, reported, or intended.

    Examples
    • The results were better than expected.
    • The project took longer than originally planned.
    • The consequences were more serious than previously reported.
    • The final sample was smaller than initially intended.
    Clarity first

    If different people had different expectations, name them: The results were better than the researchers had expected, not simply better than expected.

    A time, place, or situation

    A short time, place, or condition can stand for a longer repeated idea. For example, better than last year usually means “better than students performed last year.” Make sure the same action or quality is understood on both sides.

    Examples
    • Students performed better than last year.
    • The machine works more reliably in the laboratory than in ordinary homes.
    • The issue receives more attention during elections than at other times.
    • The second group responded less confidently under pressure than during practice.

    A number, amount, age, or limit

    After more than or less than, a number creates a boundary: above it or below it. The phrase may be a neutral limit or may carry the speaker’s reaction, depending on words such as no and the surrounding context.

    Examples
    • More than 300 students applied.
    • The journey took less than three hours.
    • Applicants must be older than 18.
    • The summary should contain not more than 100 words.

    Do not shorten a comparison into ambiguity

    Ambiguity warning

    She likes Ali more than Reza can mean either “more than she likes Reza” or “more than Reza likes Ali.” Write the missing structure when two readings are possible: She likes Ali more than she likes Reza or She likes Ali more than Reza does.

    Chapter 09

    Avoid repetition with ellipsis and substitution

    Natural comparisons rarely repeat every word on both sides. English either replaces the repeated material—substitution—or leaves predictable words unstated—ellipsis.

    Replace a repeated countable noun with one/ones

    One replaces a repeated singular countable noun; ones replaces a repeated plural countable noun. Keep any adjective that distinguishes the substitute—the old one, the rural ones. Do not normally use one for an uncountable noun such as evidence.

    Examples
    • The new proposal is more practical than the old one.
    • The final questions were harder than the earlier ones.
    • The debate became more a discussion about responsibility than one about technical ability.
    • Urban schools often have more resources than rural ones.

    Use that/those in formal comparisons

    In formal comparisons, that can replace a singular or uncountable noun phrase and those can replace a plural one. They are especially common before of, a reduced description, or who: that of the earlier study, those reported last year, those who practiced.

    Examples
    • The population of the capital is larger than that of any other city in the country.
    • The results were more reliable than those of the earlier study.
    • The environmental cost may be greater than that created by the original policy.
    • Students who practiced regularly performed better than those who did not.

    Use a possessive replacement

    A possessive form such as mine, ours, hers, or the department’s includes the missing noun, so repeating that noun is unnecessary. The intended noun must be obvious from the first side.

    Examples
    • Her conclusion is more cautious than mine.
    • Their evidence was stronger than ours.
    • The university’s response was less detailed than the department’s.

    Let the helper verb carry the repeated action

    Heavy repetition

    Students improved more rapidly than we thought they would improve.

    Natural substitution

    Students improved more rapidly than we thought they would.

    Leave out a shared description, preposition, or noun

    Examples
    • The policy is more useful for large institutions than for small businesses.
    • Students were more interested in the examples than in the theory.
    • The problem affects rural communities more severely than urban ones.
    • The change created more confusion than clarity.

    Recognize comparative deletion

    English automatically removes the repeated comparative quality from the second side:

    Hidden repeated material
    • She is taller than I am [tall].
    • We collected more evidence than they did [collect evidence].
    • The course contains fewer assignments than the previous one contained [assignments].
    Chapter 10

    Describe change: gradual growth, decline, and linked effects

    Repeat the comparative to show continuing change

    Examples
    • The problem became more and more difficult to ignore.
    • Students grew less and less dependent on printed materials.
    • The instructions became clearer and clearer with each revision.
    • Housing is becoming increasingly expensive in major cities.

    Increasingly is a useful formal alternative to more and more. For decline, English often prefers increasingly less, progressively less, or a clearer negative adjective.

    Link two changing situations with the more …, the more/less …

    the + comparative idea, the + comparative result
    Examples
    • The more carefully students analyze the question, the more relevant their answers become.
    • The less time writers spend planning, the more likely they are to repeat themselves.
    • The more examples a response includes, the less space remains for explanation.
    • The earlier a problem is identified, the easier it is to solve.

    The first the does not mean “the specific one.” In this structure, both occurrences mean roughly to that degree.

    Short fixed forms

    Examples
    • The sooner, the better.
    • The more, the merrier.
    • The less said, the better.
    • The bigger the sample, the more reliable the result.

    Use all the more/less to strengthen a reason

    Examples
    • The policy is all the more important because it protects vulnerable groups.
    • Her achievement is all the more impressive given the limited time available.
    • The proposal is all the less convincing because its evidence cannot be verified.
    • The delay made the final success all the more satisfying.
    Chapter 11

    Special meanings of more, less, and no

    In this chapter, several expressions look like ordinary comparisons but perform a different job: they limit, emphasize, dismiss, praise, or soften a judgment. Learn the meaning of the whole pattern rather than translating each word separately.

    More than can mean “not merely”

    Here more than does not compare two measured amounts. It says that the first label is incomplete: the subject includes that thing but also has a wider, deeper, or more important role.

    Examples and meanings
    • Grammar is more than a collection of rules.Rules are part of grammar, but grammar also expresses relationships and meaning.
    • The task requires more than simply mentioning an example.An example must also be explained and connected to the argument.
    • Education is more than preparation for employment; it also shapes judgment and citizenship.Job preparation is only one of education’s functions.

    More than can intensify an adjective

    Before certain adjectives, more than means “very,” “fully,” or “certainly.” There is usually no stated comparison point. This pattern is especially common with happy, willing, capable, sufficient, likely, and ready.

    Examples and meanings
    • I would be more than happy to discuss the issue.I would be very happy and completely willing.
    • The team is more than capable of completing the project.The team clearly has all the necessary ability.
    • The available evidence is more than sufficient to justify further investigation.The evidence is fully sufficient, perhaps beyond the minimum required.
    • A delay is more than likely if the funding is not approved.A delay is very likely.

    Less than + adjective often gives a controlled negative judgment

    This pattern says that something fails to reach the named positive standard. It can sound restrained and diplomatic, but context can make it sharply critical: less than honest strongly suggests dishonesty.

    Examples and meanings
    • The result was less than ideal.The result had noticeable weaknesses, expressed politely.
    • His explanation was less than convincing.The explanation did not persuade the speaker.
    • The company was less than honest about the risks.This restrained wording strongly implies that the company was dishonest.
    • The instructions were less than clear.The instructions were confusing or insufficiently clear.

    Distinguish no from not before quantities

    Both forms can describe the same numerical boundary, but they often present it differently. Not more/less than is especially useful for a neutral rule or mathematical limit. No more/less than can also state a boundary, but in ordinary prose it often adds the speaker’s reaction: “only” or “as many/much as.” Context remains important; these are tendencies, not unbreakable laws.

    no more than 50

    Common evaluative meaning: only 50—the speaker presents 50 as a small or disappointing amount. No more than fifty students attended, although the hall held five hundred.
    Possible limit meaning: it may also mean “at most 50,” especially in instructions: Use no more than fifty words.

    not more than 50

    Neutral ceiling: 50 is the maximum; 49 or fewer is acceptable. This is common in formal rules, specifications, and careful numerical statements: The response must contain not more than fifty words.

    no less than 50

    Common evaluative meaning: as many/much as 50—the speaker presents 50 as impressively or surprisingly large. No less than fifty researchers signed the letter.
    Possible minimum meaning: it can also mean “at least 50.”

    not less than 50

    Neutral floor: 50 is the minimum; 50 or more is required. This is common in formal requirements: The sample must include not less than fifty participants.

    Quick contrast

    Only twenty attended: No more than twenty students attended.   Maximum allowed: Not more than twenty students may enter.
    An impressive two hundred attended: No less than two hundred students attended.   Minimum required: Not less than two hundred responses are required.

    Nothing more than, little more than, and nothing less than

    Be nothing more than + noun means “be merely that and have no greater value or status.” It often dismisses or minimizes. Be little more than + noun is slightly less absolute: the thing has developed only a little beyond that stage. Be nothing less than + adjective/noun means “be completely or truly that,” and strongly emphasizes praise or criticism.

    Examples and meanings
    • The claim is nothing more than speculation.The speaker dismisses it as mere speculation, not reliable evidence.
    • The first version was little more than a rough outline.It had developed only slightly beyond an outline.
    • Her recovery was nothing less than remarkable.Her recovery was truly remarkable; weaker praise would be insufficient.
    • Ignoring the evidence would be nothing less than irresponsible.Ignoring it would be completely irresponsible.
    A different meaning with wants and wishes

    I want nothing more than to go home does not dismiss going home as unimportant. It means “going home is my greatest or only wish; I want nothing beyond it.” The verb and context determine the meaning.

    No more X than Y: deny the same label on both sides

    In A is no more X than B is, B is chosen as something that clearly is not X. The sentence says A is not X either. This is a logical analogy, not a small difference in degree.

    Examples and meanings
    • A whale is no more a fish than a horse is.A horse is clearly not a fish, and neither is a whale.
    • Memorizing an answer is no more evidence of understanding than copying a definition is.Neither action proves genuine understanding.
    Do not confuse it with no less

    The second method is no less effective than the first means that the second is at least equally effective. It does not deny effectiveness.

    No better/worse/different than

    Here no means “by zero degree in that direction.” No better than means not better and often implies equally poor; no worse than means not worse and may reassure; no different from means effectively the same.

    Examples and meanings
    • The copied explanation is no better than the inaccurate original.The copy offers no improvement and is probably equally poor.
    • The revised system is no worse than the one it replaced.At minimum, the revised system has not become worse.
    • His new proposal is no different from the old one.There is no meaningful difference.
    • The second group performed no less effectively than the first.Its performance was at least equally effective.

    So much the better/worse

    These fixed responses evaluate an additional fact. So much the better means the new fact makes the situation even better; so much the worse means it makes the situation even worse.

    Examples
    • If the simpler method is also cheaper, so much the better.
    • If the evidence cannot be checked independently, so much the worse for the argument.

    More or less and more often than not

    These are separate fixed expressions. More or less means “approximately” or “almost completely.” More often than not means “usually”—on more occasions than not.

    Examples and meanings
    • The work is more or less complete.It is approximately or almost complete.
    • More often than not, students lose points because their ideas are underdeveloped rather than grammatically incorrect.This is usually the reason.
    • The two versions are more or less the same in content.Their content is approximately the same, though small differences may exist.
    Chapter 12

    Choose, correct, and express preference with rather than

    Rather than contrasts two choices or explanations

    Rather than presents one choice, cause, or description as preferable or more accurate than another. It usually contrasts alternatives; it does not measure a simple difference in degree.

    Examples
    • Students should develop one idea fully rather than list several undeveloped points.
    • The problem resulted from poor planning rather than a lack of funding.
    • The response should sound thoughtful rather than memorized.
    • The policy protects established companies rather than small new businesses.

    Keep the two sides parallel

    Two nouns

    discussion rather than confrontation

    Two adjectives

    practical rather than theoretical

    Two base verbs

    explain rather than repeat

    Two -ing forms

    by explaining rather than repeating

    Two infinitives

    to explain rather than to repeat

    The second to may be omitted—to explain rather than repeat—when the structure remains clear.

    Rather than can introduce the rejected choice first

    Placing rather than first gives the rejected option special attention before the sentence presents the preferred action. A comma normally separates the opening phrase from the main statement.

    Examples
    • Rather than criticize the proposal immediately, the committee requested additional evidence.
    • Rather than being a weakness, the small sample may be appropriate for an exploratory study.
    • Rather than asking students to memorize a fixed answer, teachers should train them to adapt ideas to the question.

    Would rather … than …

    Examples
    • I would rather revise the report than submit an unclear version.
    • Most students would rather receive detailed feedback than a score alone.
    • She would rather work independently than depend on an unreliable partner.

    After would rather, use the base verb without to: would rather wait, not would rather to wait.

    Would sooner … than …

    This is a less common and somewhat stronger or more formal alternative to would rather.

    Examples
    • She would sooner postpone the project than approve it without reliable evidence.
    • I would sooner admit that I am uncertain than present a claim I cannot support.

    Rather than and instead of

    Both can reject one choice in favor of another. Rather than is especially useful for balanced contrasts and recommendations; instead of often presents a direct replacement.

    Examples
    • Students should explain the example rather than merely mention it.
    • The instructor used a diagram instead of a long verbal description.

    Would rather + person + past form

    When the preferred action belongs to another person, use would rather + person + past form. The past form creates distance and expresses a present or future preference; it is not ordinary past time.

    Examples
    • I would rather you emailed me before changing the schedule.
    • The professor would rather students developed their own examples than memorized model answers.

    The past form here expresses a present or future preference; it does not necessarily describe past time.

    Chapter 13

    Use other than and choose comparators that do not take than

    Other than meaning “except”

    Here other than means “except for.” It commonly follows negatives and broad words such as no, nothing, anyone, anything, and everywhere, where it identifies the only exception.

    Examples
    • The university offered no explanation other than a brief administrative notice.
    • Students may choose any topic other than the ones already discussed.
    • No one other than the project manager could authorize the change.
    • The room was empty other than for two chairs near the window.

    Other than meaning “different from”

    Here other than means “different from” or “not the expected thing.” This use is formal and less common than different from, but it is useful in phrases such as anything other than and other than that.

    Examples
    • The outcome was completely other than what we had expected.
    • Anything other than a full explanation would be inadequate.
    • Other than that, the proposal is well designed.

    None other than for a surprising identity

    None other than is a fixed emphatic phrase used to reveal a notable or surprising identity. It does not mean “except”; it means “the person was, surprisingly, exactly …”.

    Examples
    • The guest speaker was none other than the university president.

    Different, similar, and the same

    different from

    The safest general form: This result is different from the earlier one.

    different than

    Common before a complete idea, especially in American English: The process was different than we expected. Different from what we expected is also standard.

    similar to

    Use to, not than: The two systems are similar to each other.

    the same as

    Use as: The final result was the same as before.

    Compare with and compare to

    Modern English often allows both. Compare with is especially natural when carefully examining similarities and differences; compare to is especially natural when saying that one thing resembles another. The distinction is useful, but it is not absolute.

    Examples
    • The study compares online classes with face-to-face classes in cost, participation, and learning outcomes.
    • The professor compared the spread of misinformation to a virus that moves rapidly through a population.
    • Compared with the earlier version, the new system is easier to navigate.
    • In comparison with large universities, small colleges may offer more personal support.

    Contrast without a comparative adjective

    Examples
    • Unlike the first proposal, the second explains how the program will be funded.
    • The first group focused on speed, whereas the second emphasized accuracy.
    • Online courses offer flexibility, while classroom courses provide more immediate social interaction.
    • In contrast to the earlier policy, the revised rule includes an appeal process.

    Important comparative words that take to

    Examples
    • This method is superior to the previous one.
    • The second material is inferior to the first in durability.
    • Online submission is preferable to sending printed copies.
    • Mina is senior to me in the organization.
    • The safety review is prior to final approval.

    Use prefer X to Y, not prefer X than Y

    With nouns or -ing activities, use prefer X to Y. When comparing two intended actions with infinitives, English often uses would prefer to X rather than Y.

    Examples
    • Many students prefer examples to abstract explanations.
    • I prefer working in the morning to studying late at night.
    • I would prefer to discuss the issue privately rather than raise it during the meeting.
    Chapter 14

    Master special than patterns and logical comparisons

    No sooner … than …

    This formal structure means that the second event happened immediately after the first. When no sooner begins the sentence, use inversion.

    Examples
    • No sooner had the meeting begun than the fire alarm sounded.
    • No sooner had the policy been announced than critics began questioning its cost.
    • The meeting had no sooner begun than the connection failed.

    Sooner rather than later

    Examples
    • The university should address the housing shortage sooner rather than later.
    • Students benefit when feedback is provided sooner rather than later.

    Compare a member with the rest of its own group logically

    Logical comparison

    Write Tokyo is larger than any other city in Japan, not larger than any city in Japan, because Tokyo itself is a city in Japan. Other useful forms are larger than all other cities and larger than most cities.

    Better/easier/more useful to X than (to) Y

    Examples
    • It is better to admit uncertainty than to invent evidence.
    • It may be easier to introduce a rule than enforce it consistently.
    • For a short response, it is more useful to develop one reason fully than to mention several reasons briefly.

    Short comparison points

    Examples
    • Students have access to more information than ever before.
    • The need for reliable sources is greater than ever.
    • The process took longer than usual.
    • The final answer was shorter than necessary but clearer than before.
    Chapter 15

    Turn comparative structures into TOEFL language

    Academic Discussion frames

    Weigh two explanations

    The problem is more about ___ than about ___ because ___.

    Use this when two causes exist but one is more important.

    Correct a label

    This is not so much a ___ as a ___.

    Use this to replace a weak description with a more accurate one.

    Show linked effects

    The more ___, the more/less ___.

    Use this for cause-and-effect relationships that change by degree.

    Reject an inferior choice

    Students should ___ rather than ___.

    Use this for recommendations and priorities.

    Email frames

    Polite preference

    I would prefer to ___ rather than ___.

    Useful when suggesting an alternative without sounding aggressive.

    Explain an unexpected difficulty

    The process has taken considerably longer than I was originally told it would.

    Useful when reporting a delay precisely.

    Ask for a clearer solution

    A written explanation would be more useful than a brief verbal response.

    Useful when comparing possible forms of support.

    Limit a request

    I am requesting no more than ___.

    Use this to show that your request is modest.

    Speaking frames

    Reusable speaking frames
    • I find X more useful than Y because …
    • The main issue is less about X than about Y.
    • I would rather X than Y, mainly because …
    • The more people X, the easier/harder it becomes to Y.
    • X is more than just Y; it also …

    A practical five-step method

    1. Identify the two sides you want to compare.
    2. Decide whether you are comparing a quality, an action, a number, an amount, a label, or a choice.
    3. Choose the correct marker: -er, more, less, fewer, rather than, or another comparator.
    4. Make the two sides grammatically parallel.
    5. Remove repetition only when the shortened sentence remains completely clear.

    Editing strategy

    Underline the two sides of every long comparison. Check that they express the same type of thing: noun with noun, action with action, reason with reason, or complete idea with complete idea.

    Chapter 17

    Practice: build comparisons from clear facts

    Each item supplies the factual relationship you need. Combine the information into one natural sentence using the comparison system taught in this guide. The answer key gives one strong model; another sentence may also be correct if it preserves the same meaning and sounds natural.

    Working method

    First decide what the two sides really are: qualities, actions, amounts, labels, causes, limits, or choices. Then choose the most economical structure that keeps those two sides clear and parallel.

    Level 1 · Build ordinary comparisons

    1. The first room is 30 square metres. The second room is 42 square metres.

    2. Both policies are practical, but the revised policy can be applied in more situations and with fewer difficulties.

    3. Nadia sounded confident throughout her answer. Amir hesitated repeatedly and sounded uncertain.

    4. The old system uses 60 units of energy. The new system uses 40.

    5. The first essay contains twelve errors. The final essay contains six.

    6. The first solution works well. The second produces more accurate and stable results.

    7. We expected the task to be fairly easy. In reality, it was quite difficult.

    8. The journey takes two hours and forty minutes. Three hours is the upper boundary being discussed.

    9. Mina is 26 years old. Her brother is 23.

    10. The old figure was 100. The new figure is 120.

    11. The first explanation is clear. After revision, the second explanation is unexpectedly clearer still.

    12. The response is 380 words long. About 250 words would have communicated the idea fully.

    Level 2 · Compare descriptions, actions, and quantities

    1. Students are strongly worried about housing costs. They are worried about tuition fees too, but to a lower degree.

    2. Installing the software takes five minutes. Maintaining it requires specialist knowledge and regular work.

    3. The policy causes serious difficulties for small businesses. Large corporations experience much milder effects.

    4. The first report states its position directly and supports it with strong evidence. The second is indirect and weakly supported.

    5. The old course gave students eight speaking tasks. The new course gives them twelve.

    6. The original survey contained 40 questions and took about 20 minutes. The new survey contains 25 questions and takes about 12 minutes.

    7. The complaint is not isolated: three students have now complained about the schedule.

    8. The first essay scored 24. The second scored 27.

    9. The original cost estimate was €10,000. The final cost was €8,000.

    10. The pilot system uses 30 kilowatt-hours. The full system uses 90.

    11. The article presents the issue as simple. The real issue contains many interacting causes and consequences.

    12. The first result is 51.0. The second is 51.2, a difference too small to be practically important.

    Level 3 · Classify accurately and avoid repetition

    1. She did feel some anger, but disappointment was clearly her main emotion.

    2. The proposal aims to transform the entire system, but it gives little attention to cost or implementation.

    3. The meeting was designed to impress voters and contained very little genuine exchange of ideas.

    4. The book mainly reflects on memory and identity; only secondarily does it record historical events.

    5. Students expected the course to dominate their week. It required effort, but it was much more manageable than they feared.

    6. People often call the problem financial. Money matters, but weak administration is the main source of difficulty.

    7. The answer contains a few factual mistakes, but its central weakness is that it does not address the question.

    8. The policy design had weaknesses, but the policy mainly failed because officials implemented it poorly.

    9. Evidence is discussed in the debate, but disagreements about values are much more central.

    10. The revised proposal is practical. The earlier proposal is much less practical.

    11. The current study produced highly reliable results. The earlier study produced less reliable results.

    12. Her evidence is strong. The evidence collected by our team is weaker.

    13. Over the same twelve-week period, students improved by 18 percent. Researchers had predicted an improvement of only 5 percent.

    14. Sara consistently works 45 hours a week. Reza works 35.

    15. Researchers predicted modest gains. The actual results were substantially stronger.

    16. Leila likes Ali a great deal. She likes Reza less; the comparison concerns Leila’s feelings toward the two men, not Reza’s feelings.

    Level 4 · Interpret limits and special meanings

    1. Grammar includes rules, but it also enables speakers to express relationships, emphasis, attitude, and meaning.

    2. The team has all the skills, experience, and resources required to complete the work.

    3. The result can be used, but it falls clearly below the ideal standard and has several weaknesses.

    4. The hall could hold 500 people, but only 20 attended, a surprisingly disappointing number.

    5. The official rule sets 50 words as the maximum. It makes no judgment about whether 50 is a large or small number.

    6. Organizers expected about 50 students, but 200 attended, an unexpectedly large number.

    7. A valid sample must contain at least 50 participants. The requirement is a neutral technical minimum.

    8. The claim has no supporting data or verified source. It is merely speculation.

    9. The first draft contains headings and a few notes but almost no developed content.

    10. Her recovery exceeded every medical expectation and deserves the strongest praise.

    11. After years abroad, his greatest wish was simply to return home; he wanted no additional reward.

    12. Each revision improved the explanation, and the improvement continued through several versions.

    13. As students devote more care to planning, the relevance of their answers increases at the same time.

    14. As writers reduce the time they spend planning, their likelihood of repeating themselves increases.

    15. The policy is important already. The fact that it protects vulnerable groups makes its importance even greater.

    16. In most cases, students need stronger idea development; complicated vocabulary is usually not the real need.

    Level 5 · Build advanced TOEFL-ready comparisons

    1. Students should develop one relevant reason fully. Listing several weak reasons is the less effective choice.

    2. The committee chose to request additional evidence. It deliberately avoided criticizing the proposal immediately.

    3. I prefer revising the report. Submitting the unclear version is unacceptable to me.

    4. My preference is that you email me before you make any change to the schedule.

    5. The university provided a brief administrative notice and absolutely no other explanation.

    6. We expected the figure to rise. Instead, it fell significantly.

    7. The new method exceeds the old method in both accuracy and speed.

    8. When given a choice, students consistently choose concrete examples instead of abstract explanations.

    9. The meeting began. Immediately afterward, the internet connection failed.

    10. Tokyo is a city in Japan, and every other Japanese city has a smaller population.

    11. For a short response, fully developing one reason produces more value than briefly listing several reasons.

    12. The current article presents weak evidence. The earlier study presented much stronger evidence, and the comparison is specifically between the evidence in the two sources.

      The two action phrases are parallel.

    The second course contains more lectures than the first does but fewer opportunities for students to apply the ideas they have learned.

    The helper verb replaces contains lectures.

    Changing comparisons

    41

    As the semester progressed, students became more and more willing to challenge weak arguments and less and less dependent on memorized phrases.

    Repeated comparative forms show two gradual changes in opposite directions.

    42

    The more carefully a writer defines the comparison point, the less likely the sentence is to become ambiguous, especially when several people or actions are mentioned.

    The two changing degrees are linked directly.

    43

    The fewer examples students try to include, the more space they have to explain the examples they actually use.

    A decreasing count produces an increasing opportunity.

    44

    The earlier a misunderstanding is corrected, the easier it is to prevent that misunderstanding from shaping the rest of the discussion.

    Two -er comparatives form a cause-and-effect relationship.

    45

    The pressure to verify online information is all the greater because misleading claims now spread faster than corrections do.

    All the greater because says the reason strengthens the need.

    46

    Her success was all the more impressive given that she completed the research with fewer resources than any other team received.

    A circumstance strengthens the evaluation, followed by a logical group comparison.

    47

    The more complicated the scoring system becomes, the less transparent it appears, and the less transparent it appears, the more difficult it becomes to trust.

    Two linked correlative comparisons form a chain of effects.

    48

    Because every unnecessary sentence gives the reader more information to process, the shorter and more focused the response, the better.

    A compact fixed-style correlative gives a practical writing principle.

    Rather than and other than

    49

    Students should adapt a flexible idea to the exact question rather than force a memorized answer into a discussion where it does not belong.

    Two base-verb phrases are contrasted after should.

    50

    Rather than dismiss the surprising result as an error, the researchers repeated the experiment under stricter conditions.

    The rejected action appears first, followed by the preferred action.

    51

    I would rather receive a direct explanation than continue guessing why the application has not been processed.

    Would rather is followed by parallel base verbs.

    52

    The instructor would rather students used a simple relevant example than repeated an impressive but unrelated one.

    Past forms express the instructor’s present preference about other people’s behavior.

    53

    The university offered no evidence other than a short internal report whose methods had never been independently reviewed.

    Other than means except.

    54

    Anything other than a complete correction would leave students with the same misunderstanding that caused the original error.

    Anything other than rejects all alternatives except the named one.

    55

    The keynote speaker was none other than the researcher whose work had inspired the entire project.

    None other than introduces a surprising identity.

    56

    Other than the lack of a detailed timetable, the proposal is more or less ready for approval.

    Other than marks an exception; more or less means approximately.

    Comparators with to/from/as and special than patterns

    57

    Although the new procedure is superior to the old one in speed, it is inferior to it in transparency and therefore not clearly preferable overall.

    Several comparative adjectives correctly take to, not than.

    58

    Most students prefer a short explanation with a clear example to a long definition without context.

    Prefer X to Y directly compares two choices.

    59

    The final outcome was different from what the committee had predicted but remarkably similar to what independent researchers had reported.

    Different from and similar to take different connectors.

    60

    The revised figures are the same as those published last month, although the explanation accompanying them is considerably clearer than before.

    The same as combines with formal substitution and a reduced time comparison.

    61

    No sooner had the university announced the new rule than students began asking whether it would apply to courses already in progress.

    Sentence-initial no sooner triggers inversion.

    62

    Because the safety issue affects thousands of commuters every day, the city should resolve it sooner rather than later.

    The fixed phrase urges prompt action.

    63

    The capital has a larger population than any other city in the country, but several smaller cities provide more reliable public services than it does.

    Other prevents an illogical self-comparison; does avoids repetition.

    64

    It is better to acknowledge a limitation than to hide it, because a cautious argument is usually more credible than an unrealistically certain one.

    Parallel infinitives are followed by a second comparison using one.

    Chapter 19

    A plain-English grammar toolbox

    These definitions explain the grammar words used throughout the guide. They are written for learners rather than grammar specialists.

    Comparative form
    A form that presents one person, thing, action, amount, or idea as higher, lower, larger, smaller, better, worse, or otherwise different in degree: taller, more useful, less carefully, fewer mistakes.
    Comparison point
    The person, thing, action, time, number, expectation, or complete idea introduced by than: than Sara, than before, than expected, or than we thought it would.
    Adjective
    A word or group of words that describes a person or thing: large, useful, difficult to explain, concerned about costs. Comparative adjectives include larger, more useful, less difficult.
    Adverb
    A word or group of words that describes how an action happens: carefully, rapidly, with confidence. Comparative adverbs include more carefully, faster, less confidently.
    Noun
    A word for a person, thing, place, idea, event, or substance: student, proposal, city, fairness, meeting, evidence.
    Countable noun
    A noun you can count separately: one student, two students; one error, five errors. Use fewer for a lower number and one/ones as replacements.
    Uncountable noun
    A noun normally treated as an amount rather than separate items: information, evidence, traffic, time. Use less for a lower amount.
    Measurement
    A total of time, money, distance, weight, or another measured quantity. English normally uses less than with the total: less than three hours; less than €50.
    Modifier
    A word or phrase that changes the strength or detail of another expression. Comparative modifiers include far, much, slightly, considerably, even, still: far more useful; slightly better.
    Complement
    Words that complete the meaning of another word. In concerned about costs, the phrase about costs completes concerned. Keep it inside the comparison when necessary.
    Preposition
    A short linking word such as about, for, to, in, with, from. Long comparisons often compare parallel preposition phrases: more useful for teachers than for students.
    Infinitive
    The form to + base verb: to explain, to apply, to maintain. Comparative sentences often place two infinitives in parallel: easier to understand than to use.
    Gerund or -ing form used as a noun
    An -ing form that names an activity: reading, planning, working independently. Keep two activities parallel: prefer reading to watching; by explaining rather than repeating.
    Parallelism
    Using matching grammatical shapes for ideas that perform the same job. Compare noun with noun, adjective with adjective, reason with reason, or action with action.
    Clause or complete idea
    A group of words built around a subject and a verb: I am, experts predicted, the article suggests, we thought it would. It may be a whole sentence or part of one.
    Helper verb
    A short verb such as do, has, can, will, would that carries tense or replaces a repeated action: She works harder than I do; It cost more than we thought it would.
    Ellipsis
    Leaving out words that the reader can recover easily: more useful for teachers than for students leaves out the repeated words it is useful.
    Substitution
    Replacing repeated material with a shorter form such as one/ones, that/those, mine/ours, or a helper verb.
    One and ones
    Replacement words for singular and plural countable nouns: the old proposal → the old one; the earlier questions → the earlier ones.
    That and those as replacements
    Formal replacements common in academic comparison: the impact of X → that of X; the results of X → those of X.
    Possessive replacement
    A form such as mine, yours, hers, ours, theirs, or a noun with ’s, that replaces an owned noun: Her argument is stronger than mine.
    Reduced ending
    A short ending such as than expected, than originally planned, than before, where the missing words are obvious from context.
    Comparative deletion
    The automatic omission of repeated comparative material after than: She is taller than I am [tall]. English does not repeat the adjective on the second side.
    Partitive phrase
    A phrase that refers to part of a larger whole: more of the money, less of the problem. This is different from the classification pattern more of a problem than a solution.
    Inversion
    A formal word-order change in which a helper verb comes before the subject: No sooner had the meeting begun than …. The order resembles a question, but the sentence is a statement.
    Register
    The level of formality and style. More of a problem than is widely natural; more a problem than is more compressed and formal.
    Ambiguity
    A sentence has ambiguity when it allows two meanings. She likes Ali more than Reza is ambiguous until the missing verb relationship is stated.
    Correlative comparison
    A paired structure showing that two degrees change together: The more carefully you plan, the clearer your answer becomes.
    Final reminder

    Every complex comparison becomes easier when you ask three questions: What two things am I comparing? What exact difference or preference am I expressing? Have I made the two sides clear and parallel?

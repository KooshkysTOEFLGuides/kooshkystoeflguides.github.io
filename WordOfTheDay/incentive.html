TOEFL Word of the Day

incentive

/ɪnˈsen.tɪv/

An incentive is something that encourages a person, group, company, or government to do something. TOEFL learners often see this word in topics about education, business, economics, environmental policy, work, and social behavior.

Meaning

01 noun · countable, often followed by to + verb or for + noun/gerund /ɪnˈsen.tɪv/

Something, often money, a reward, or a benefit, that makes someone want to do something.

Core idea: a reason or reward that pushes someone to act.

Examples

  • The company offered employees a financial incentive to work on difficult weekend projects.
  • Higher grades can be a strong incentive for students to review their notes every day.
  • Tax cuts gave businesses an incentive to invest in cleaner technology.
  • Some cities provide incentives for people to use public transportation instead of cars.
  • Without a clear incentive, many people may not change their daily habits.

Nuance and implication

Incentive is neutral and common in academic, business, and policy writing. It often suggests that behavior can be influenced by rewards, benefits, or practical advantages. It does not always mean money; praise, grades, convenience, safety, or social approval can also be incentives.

Grammar notes

  • Incentive is usually countable: an incentive, several incentives.
  • Common pattern: an incentive to + verb: an incentive to study harder.
  • Common pattern: an incentive for + person/group + to + verb: an incentive for companies to reduce waste.
  • Common pattern: an incentive for + noun/gerund: an incentive for innovation, an incentive for saving energy.
  • Common adjectives include financial, economic, strong, powerful, clear, and additional.

Common wrong usages

❌ The school gave students an incentive studying harder.

✅ The school gave students an incentive to study harder.

Explanation: Use an incentive to + verb, not an incentive + -ing directly.

❌ Money is not the only incentive of people to work hard.

✅ Money is not the only incentive for people to work hard.

Explanation: Use incentive for someone to do something.

Word Family

Form Part of speech Simple definition Examples Usage note
incentive noun Something that encourages action.
  • The bonus was an incentive to finish the project early.
  • Governments can use incentives to reduce pollution.
 The most common and TOEFL-useful form.
incentivize verb To encourage someone to do something by offering a reward or benefit.
  • The policy is designed to incentivize companies to hire more workers.
  • Schools can incentivize reading by giving students access to better materials.
 Common in business and policy writing, but slightly more formal than “encourage.”
incentivization noun The act or system of encouraging behavior through incentives.
  • The incentivization of clean energy helped the industry grow.
  • Some experts criticize the incentivization of short-term test scores.
 Formal and less common; useful mainly in academic or policy contexts.

Useful Collocations

Collocation Simple meaning Natural example
financial incentive Money or a money-related benefit that encourages action. The government offered financial incentives for families to install solar panels.
economic incentive A money or market reason that affects behavior. Lower energy costs create an economic incentive for factories to modernize their equipment.
strong incentive A powerful reason to do something. Scholarships provide a strong incentive for students to maintain high grades.
little incentive Not much reason or reward for doing something. If salaries remain low, teachers may have little incentive to stay in the profession.
provide an incentive Give someone a reason or reward to act. Free training programs provide an incentive for workers to learn new skills.
create an incentive Make a situation where people are encouraged to act. Higher prices can create an incentive for consumers to buy less.
incentive to change A reason that encourages change. Public pressure gave the company an incentive to change its labor policies.
tax incentive A tax benefit that encourages a behavior. Tax incentives encouraged homeowners to improve energy efficiency.

Synonyms and Antonyms

Meaning: something that encourages action

Type Word or phrase Nuance
Synonym motivation A more general word; it can be internal, like interest or ambition, while an incentive is often external.
Synonym encouragement Often more emotional or social; it can mean support, not necessarily a reward.
Synonym reward A reward is something received after action; an incentive is offered to encourage the action before it happens.
Synonym stimulus More formal and academic; it means something that causes a reaction or activity.
Antonym disincentive Something that discourages action or makes people less likely to do something.
Antonym deterrent Something that prevents action, often by fear, risk, cost, or punishment.
Antonym obstacle Something that makes action harder, but it does not always actively discourage people.

Etymology

Incentive comes from Latin roots connected with “setting the tune” or “encouraging.” In modern English, it means something that encourages action, especially a reward, benefit, or practical reason.

Answer Key

1. Fill in the blank

a. incentivize — The verb means “to encourage someone by offering a reward or benefit.”

b. incentive — The sentence needs a noun after “a strong.”

c. incentivization — The sentence needs a formal noun meaning “the process of encouraging through incentives.”

2. Meaning in context

B. A benefit that encourages people to do something. — The tax reduction encourages homeowners to install better windows.

3. Correct the wrong usage

a. The scholarship gave students an incentive to apply to the program. — Use incentive to + verb.

b. There is no incentive for workers to stay if the salary is too low. — Use incentive for someone to do something.

4. Natural or unnatural?

a. Natural. — “An incentive to improve” is a correct and common pattern.

b. Unnatural. — You can incentivize people or behavior, not “the exam” in this meaning.

c. Natural. — “Create an incentive for people to use...” is a correct academic pattern.

5. Best collocation

A. tax incentive. — This is a common collocation meaning a tax benefit used to encourage behavior.

6. Sample answer

Universities can create an incentive for students to do research by offering small grants and academic credit.

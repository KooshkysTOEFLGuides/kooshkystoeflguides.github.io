Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

Happening by fortunate chance

adjective /ˌserənˈdɪpətəs/ More common in formal, academic, and literary English than in everyday conversation.

happening or being discovered by chance in a way that is interesting, useful, or pleasant

In simpler words: happening by lucky chance

Typical pattern: serendipitous + noun

Natural examples

  1. A serendipitous conversation at the conference led the researchers to begin a joint project.
  2. The drug's potential use as a treatment emerged from a serendipitous laboratory observation.
  3. While browsing the archive, the historian made a serendipitous discovery that changed her interpretation of the event.
  4. Our meeting was entirely serendipitous; neither of us had planned to visit the museum that day.
  5. The student found a valuable source through a serendipitous online search.
  6. A wrong turn led to a serendipitous encounter with a quiet village.
  7. The team benefited from a serendipitous connection between two apparently unrelated ideas.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The event is not planned, but it produces an interesting, useful, or pleasant result.

Usual implication: It usually suggests a positive surprise rather than a merely random event.

Compared with “accidental”: Accidental only means unplanned and may describe something neutral or harmful. Serendipitous usually suggests that the chance event has a welcome or valuable result.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it to describe a noun, or place it after a form of be. For example: a serendipitous discovery; The discovery was serendipitous.
What can follow it
It commonly comes before nouns such as discovery, encounter, finding, meeting, connection, and event.
Position in a sentence
It usually appears before a noun, but it can also appear after be, seem, or feel.

Useful patterns

serendipitous + noun

Use this pattern to describe an unplanned event, meeting, or discovery with a welcome result.

Example: The researchers made a serendipitous discovery while testing a different theory.

be + serendipitous

Use this pattern when the adjective describes the subject of the sentence.

Example: Their encounter was completely serendipitous.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The scientist serendipitous discovered the effect.

Correct: The scientist serendipitously discovered the effect.

Use serendipitously to describe how an action happened.

Wrong: Their discovery was a serendipitous.

Correct: Their discovery was serendipitous.

Serendipitous is an adjective, not a countable noun, so do not use a before it when no noun follows.

Wrong: The equipment failure was serendipitous because it destroyed the entire experiment.

Correct: The equipment failure was accidental and destroyed the entire experiment.

Serendipitous normally suggests an interesting, useful, or pleasant result, not simply an unplanned harmful event.

Morphology

Word family

Only common and educationally useful family members are included.

uncountable noun

serendipity

/ˌserənˈdɪpəti/

the occurrence of finding something interesting, useful, or pleasant by chance

Relationship: Serendipity is the noun from which serendipitous is formed.

Pattern: by serendipity or a piece of serendipity

Usage note: Serendipity is normally uncountable. Say serendipity or a piece of serendipity, not a serendipity.

  1. The discovery of the old letters was a remarkable piece of serendipity.
  2. Serendipity sometimes plays an important role in scientific research.
  3. By pure serendipity, she met a former classmate who could help with the project.

adverb

serendipitously

/ˌserənˈdɪpətəsli/

by chance in a way that produces an interesting, useful, or pleasant result

Relationship: Serendipitously is the adverb form of serendipitous.

Pattern: verb + serendipitously

Usage note: Use it to describe how an event, meeting, or discovery happened.

  1. The two researchers met serendipitously at a workshop.
  2. The solution was discovered serendipitously during an unrelated experiment.

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

fortuitous

adjective · Happening by fortunate chance

Shared meaning: Both words can describe an unplanned event with a favorable result.

Difference: Fortuitous also means happening by chance and often describes a favorable result. Serendipitous more strongly suggests an unexpected discovery, meeting, or connection that proves valuable or pleasant.

Example: A fortuitous delay allowed the team to correct a serious error.

unexpected

adjective · Happening by fortunate chance

Shared meaning: Both words can describe something that was not planned or predicted.

Difference: Unexpected only means not expected. It can describe good, bad, or neutral events and does not necessarily suggest a valuable chance discovery.

Example: The unexpected storm forced the researchers to leave the site early.

fortunate

adjective · Happening by fortunate chance

Shared meaning: Both words often describe a positive result.

Difference: Fortunate means lucky or favorable, but the event does not have to be unplanned or involve discovering something by chance.

Example: It was fortunate that the backup files had been saved.

chance

adjective · Happening by fortunate chance

Shared meaning: Both can describe something that happens without planning.

Difference: Chance simply means accidental or unplanned. It does not by itself suggest that the result is useful, interesting, or pleasant.

Example: A chance remark gave the journalist a new lead.

Word history

Etymology

Only confidently documented details are shown.

Origin

Serendipitous was formed from serendipity, a word coined by the English writer Horace Walpole in 1754 after the tale The Three Princes of Serendip.

Historical form

Serendip was an old name for Sri Lanka.

Development

The adjective came to describe events or discoveries that happen by chance and lead to an interesting, useful, or pleasant result.

Memory link

Meanings and usage

Meaning 1

Easily affected or harmed

adjective /səˈsɛptəbəl/ Common in academic, scientific, medical, environmental, and formal English.

easily affected or harmed by a disease, condition, substance, or environmental influence

In simpler words: easily harmed or affected

Typical pattern: be susceptible to + noun or -ing form

Natural examples

  1. Young children are particularly susceptible to respiratory infections.
  2. Coastal communities are highly susceptible to flooding during severe storms.
  3. Plants grown in poor soil may be more susceptible to disease.
  4. Older adults are often more susceptible to the effects of extreme heat.
  5. The crop is susceptible to damage from several common insects.
  6. People with weakened immune systems may be susceptible to infections that rarely affect healthy adults.
  7. Certain metals are susceptible to corrosion when exposed to salt water.
  8. The region's dry forests are increasingly susceptible to wildfires.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The word suggests a weakness or lack of protection that makes harm or change more likely.

Usual implication: The person or thing is at greater risk than others under the same conditions.

Compared with “vulnerable”: Vulnerable broadly means open to harm or attack. Susceptible usually identifies the specific disease, influence, or condition that can affect someone or something.

Grammar notes

How to use it
It most often appears after a form of be, as in The plant is susceptible to disease.
What can follow it
It is usually followed by to and then a noun or an -ing form.
Common preposition
Use susceptible to, not susceptible for or susceptible of, in ordinary modern English. For example: susceptible to infection or susceptible to being damaged.
Position in a sentence
It can also appear before a noun, especially in expressions such as susceptible individuals or susceptible species.

Useful patterns

be susceptible to + disease or condition

Use this pattern when someone or something can easily develop a disease or harmful condition.

Example: These trees are susceptible to a fungal disease.

be susceptible to + damage or change

Use this pattern when something can easily be harmed or altered.

Example: The ancient paper is highly susceptible to moisture damage.

particularly or highly susceptible to + noun

Use particularly or highly to emphasize a greater level of risk.

Example: Newborn animals are particularly susceptible to cold temperatures.

susceptible + person, group, species, or material

Use this pattern before a noun when identifying those most likely to be affected.

Example: The vaccine was offered first to susceptible groups.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: Children are susceptible for this infection.

Correct: Children are susceptible to this infection.

Use susceptible to before the disease or influence.

Wrong: The plant is susceptible of being damaged by frost.

Correct: The plant is susceptible to being damaged by frost.

Susceptible to is the normal learner-friendly pattern in modern English.

Wrong: This material can susceptible to heat.

Correct: This material can be susceptible to heat.

Use be after can because susceptible is an adjective.

Wrong: The disease is susceptible to young children.

Correct: Young children are susceptible to the disease.

The person or thing that can be affected is the subject of the sentence.

Meaning 2

Easily influenced

adjective /səˈsɛptəbəl/ Common in psychology, sociology, education, marketing, politics, and formal discussion.

easily influenced, persuaded, or emotionally affected by other people, ideas, or experiences

In simpler words: easily influenced or persuaded

Typical pattern: be susceptible to + influence, pressure, persuasion, or emotion

Natural examples

  1. Young consumers may be especially susceptible to online advertising.
  2. People who feel socially isolated can be more susceptible to pressure from a group.
  3. He was susceptible to flattery and often trusted people too quickly.
  4. Children may be susceptible to the attitudes expressed by adults around them.
  5. Investors can become susceptible to panic when financial markets fall suddenly.
  6. The campaign targeted voters who were considered susceptible to emotional messages.
  7. She is highly susceptible to criticism and may interpret neutral comments negatively.
  8. Individuals who lack reliable information may be more susceptible to misleading claims.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The person is more likely than others to have their beliefs, decisions, or emotions changed by an outside influence.

Usual implication: This sense often suggests limited resistance, awareness, experience, or confidence.

Compared with “receptive”: Receptive usually means willing to consider an idea and is often positive. Susceptible suggests that someone may be influenced too easily and is often negative.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it after be and before to, as in She is susceptible to pressure.
What can follow it
It commonly appears with advertising, influence, pressure, persuasion, flattery, criticism, fear, and manipulation.
Common preposition
Use susceptible to something or susceptible to being influenced.
Position in a sentence
It usually appears after be, become, seem, or remain.

Useful patterns

be susceptible to + influence or pressure

Use this pattern when someone's opinions or behavior can easily be changed.

Example: Inexperienced employees may be susceptible to pressure from senior colleagues.

be susceptible to + emotional reaction

Use this pattern when someone is easily affected by a particular feeling or emotional experience.

Example: He is susceptible to anxiety during periods of uncertainty.

make someone susceptible to + noun

Use this pattern when a condition increases a person's chance of being influenced.

Example: A lack of confidence can make people susceptible to manipulation.

more susceptible to + noun than + comparison

Use this pattern to compare how easily different people are influenced.

Example: Adolescents may be more susceptible to peer pressure than adults.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: He is susceptible by other people's opinions.

Correct: He is susceptible to other people's opinions.

Use to after susceptible.

Wrong: Advertising can susceptible young consumers.

Correct: Advertising can influence young consumers.

Susceptible is an adjective, not a verb.

Wrong: She is very susceptible for flattery.

Correct: She is very susceptible to flattery.

The correct preposition is to.

Wrong: Social isolation may make people susceptible for being manipulated.

Correct: Social isolation may make people susceptible to being manipulated.

Use susceptible to followed by an -ing form.

Meaning 3

Open to change or interpretation

adjective /səˈsɛptəbəl/ Common in academic, legal, scientific, and analytical writing.

able to be affected, changed, misunderstood, or interpreted in a particular way

In simpler words: able to be affected or understood in a certain way

Typical pattern: be susceptible to + interpretation, bias, error, change, or damage

Natural examples

  1. The results are susceptible to several different interpretations.
  2. Self-reported data are susceptible to bias because participants may not remember events accurately.
  3. The material is susceptible to damage when exposed to extreme heat.
  4. The original wording was susceptible to misunderstanding.
  5. Complex computer systems may be susceptible to human error.
  6. The method is susceptible to small variations in temperature.
  7. The agreement was written clearly so that it would not be susceptible to conflicting interpretations.
  8. Historical evidence is often susceptible to revision when new information becomes available.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: Something contains features that allow a particular effect, problem, or interpretation to occur.

Usual implication: The result may depend on outside conditions, human judgment, or the way information is collected or expressed.

Compared with “subject to”: Subject to can mean likely to experience something or controlled by a rule. Susceptible to emphasizes that the nature of something makes it easily affected or open to a particular result.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it after be and before to, as in The data are susceptible to error.
What can follow it
It often appears with interpretation, misunderstanding, bias, error, variation, change, damage, and manipulation.
Common preposition
Use susceptible to followed by a noun or an -ing form.
Position in a sentence
This formal sense is most common after a form of be.

Useful patterns

be susceptible to interpretation

Use this pattern when words, evidence, or results can be understood in more than one way.

Example: The final sentence is susceptible to two very different interpretations.

be susceptible to bias or error

Use this pattern when a method or result can easily be influenced by mistakes or unfair judgment.

Example: Interviews are susceptible to bias if the questions are not carefully designed.

be susceptible to change or variation

Use this pattern when something can easily be altered by outside conditions.

Example: The measurements are susceptible to variation caused by humidity.

be susceptible to being + past participle

Use this pattern when something can easily undergo a particular action.

Example: The figures are susceptible to being misinterpreted.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The results are susceptible for several interpretations.

Correct: The results are susceptible to several interpretations.

Use to before the possible interpretation or effect.

Wrong: The method is susceptible to make errors.

Correct: The method is susceptible to error.

Use a noun after to here.

Wrong: The wording is susceptible to misunderstand.

Correct: The wording is susceptible to misunderstanding.

After to, use the noun misunderstanding in this pattern.

Wrong: The data susceptible to bias should be checked carefully.

Correct: The data that are susceptible to bias should be checked carefully.

Add that are to make the sentence clear and natural for learners.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

vulnerable

adjective · Easily affected or harmed

Shared meaning: Both words describe a lack of protection against harm.

Difference: Vulnerable broadly means open to physical, emotional, or social harm. Susceptible usually identifies a specific disease, condition, or influence that may cause harm.

Example: Small island communities are vulnerable to rising sea levels.

prone

adjective · Easily affected or harmed

Shared meaning: Both words indicate that a problem or condition is more likely.

Difference: Prone to means likely to experience something repeatedly or have a particular tendency. Susceptible to emphasizes that someone or something can easily be affected by an outside cause.

Example: The region is prone to severe droughts.

impressionable

adjective · Easily influenced

Shared meaning: Both words can describe someone who is easily influenced.

Difference: Impressionable mainly describes people, especially young people, whose opinions and behavior are easily shaped. Susceptible is broader and can describe influence, pressure, disease, damage, or bias.

Example: The program was criticized for targeting impressionable teenagers.

sensitive

adjective · Easily affected or harmed

Shared meaning: Both words can describe a strong reaction to outside conditions.

Difference: Sensitive can mean quick to notice or react to something and is not always negative. Susceptible means likely to be affected or harmed by a particular cause.

Example: Some plants are sensitive to changes in soil temperature.

Direct or useful antonyms

resistant

adjective · all senses

Opposition: Resistant describes someone or something that is not easily affected, damaged, persuaded, or changed.

Usage note: Use resistant to, followed by the disease, influence, substance, or change.

Example: This crop is resistant to several common diseases.

immune

adjective · Easily affected or harmed

Opposition: Immune describes a person or organism protected against a particular disease, or something completely unaffected by a particular influence.

Usage note: Immune is stronger than resistant and often suggests complete protection.

Example: Most adults are immune to the disease after vaccination.

Precision

Words learners may confuse

sensitive

adjective

Why it is confusing: Both words can describe a strong reaction to outside conditions, but sensitive does not necessarily imply weakness or harm.

Key difference: Susceptible means likely to be affected or harmed. Sensitive often means quick to notice, feel, or react to something and may describe a useful ability.

susceptible: The plant is susceptible to fungal disease.

sensitive: The instrument is sensitive enough to detect tiny changes in pressure.

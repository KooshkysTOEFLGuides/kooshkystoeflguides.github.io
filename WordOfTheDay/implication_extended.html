Meanings & Usage

Meaning 1 countable noun, often plural /ˌɪm.pləˈkeɪ.ʃən/

A possible result, effect, or consequence of an action, decision, event, or idea.

Natural examples

  1. The discovery has major implications for cancer research.
  2. Before adopting the policy, officials considered its economic implications.
  3. The study explores the long-term implications of remote work for urban life.
  4. Rising sea levels have serious implications for coastal communities.
  5. The professor asked students to think about the broader implications of the experiment.

Nuance & implication

This meaning is more academic than simple words like “effect” or “result.” It often suggests that the result is important, indirect, or worth thinking about carefully. In TOEFL texts, writers often discuss the social, economic, scientific, or environmental implications of a finding or policy.

Grammar notes

In this meaning, implication is countable and very often plural: the implications of something or implications for something. Use of for the source and for for the affected area or group.

Common wrong usage

  • ❌ This policy has many implication. → ✅ This policy has many implications.
  • ❌ The implication on students is serious. → ✅ The implications for students are serious.

TOEFL tip

In Reading, implications often asks you to understand why a detail matters beyond the immediate sentence. In Writing, it is useful for explaining consequences: “This has important implications for education.”

Meaning 2 countable or uncountable noun /ˌɪm.pləˈkeɪ.ʃən/

A meaning, idea, or suggestion that is not stated directly but can be understood from what someone says or writes.

Natural examples

  1. The implication of her comment was that the project needed better leadership.
  2. He did not criticize the plan directly, but the implication was clear.
  3. The article carries the implication that technology alone cannot solve the problem.
  4. By implication, the speaker suggested that older methods were no longer useful.
  5. Students should pay attention to the implication of a contrast word like “however.”

Nuance & implication

This meaning is about indirect meaning. The speaker or writer may not say something openly, but the listener or reader can infer it. It is close to “suggestion,” but it sounds more formal and analytical.

Grammar notes

Common patterns include the implication is that + clause, the implication of + noun/comment/statement, and by implication. Do not confuse the noun implication with the verb imply.

Common wrong usage

  • ❌ The author implications that the theory is weak. → ✅ The author implies that the theory is weak.
  • ❌ The implication is the economy will improve. → ✅ The implication is that the economy will improve.

TOEFL tip

This meaning is very useful for inference questions. When a question asks what a professor “implies,” it is asking for an implication, not a sentence that was stated directly.

Meaning 3 uncountable noun /ˌɪm.pləˈkeɪ.ʃən/

The fact or suggestion that someone is involved in something wrong, illegal, or harmful.

Natural examples

  1. The official denied any implication in the financial scandal.
  2. The report raised questions about the company’s implication in illegal dumping.
  3. Several documents suggested his implication in the decision to hide the evidence.
  4. The journalist was careful not to make an unfair implication of guilt.
  5. The investigation found no clear implication of the researchers in the data manipulation.

Nuance & implication

This meaning is usually negative and formal. It does not always prove guilt; it can mean that someone appears connected to a bad action. Because it is serious, it is often used in legal, political, or journalistic contexts.

Grammar notes

This meaning is commonly used with in: someone’s implication in a scandal/crime. You may also see implication of guilt. The related verb is implicate: “The evidence implicated several officials.”

Common wrong usage

  • ❌ The evidence implication the official. → ✅ The evidence implicated the official.
  • ❌ His implication to the crime was denied. → ✅ His implication in the crime was denied.

TOEFL tip

This meaning is less common than the first two, but it may appear in passages about politics, history, law, or public controversy. Notice the negative context around words like “scandal,” “crime,” “guilt,” or “investigation.”

Word Family

imply

Part of speech: transitive verb

Definition: to suggest something without saying it directly.

  1. The professor implied that the results were not reliable.
  2. The data imply a connection between sleep and memory.

implicate

Part of speech: transitive verb

Definition: to show or suggest that someone or something is involved in something bad or harmful.

  1. The documents implicated several officials in the scandal.
  2. New evidence may implicate the factory in the pollution case.

implied

Part of speech: adjective

Definition: suggested but not said directly.

  1. The implied meaning of the sentence is that the plan failed.
  2. TOEFL inference questions often test implied information.

implicit

Part of speech: adjective

Definition: understood without being stated directly; hidden inside the meaning of something.

  1. The agreement included an implicit promise of cooperation.
  2. The author’s criticism is implicit rather than direct.

implicitly

Part of speech: adverb

Definition: in a way that is suggested but not directly stated.

  1. The speaker implicitly criticized the government’s response.
  2. The passage implicitly supports the second theory.

explicit

Part of speech: adjective

Definition: stated clearly and directly.

  1. The instructions were explicit and easy to follow.
  2. The author makes an explicit comparison between the two theories.

Common Collocations

important implications

Meaning: serious or meaningful possible results.

Example: The findings have important implications for public health.

major implications

Meaning: very large or significant effects.

Example: The new law could have major implications for small businesses.

serious implications

Meaning: possible results that may be harmful or difficult to manage.

Example: Water shortages have serious implications for agriculture.

broader implications

Meaning: effects that go beyond the immediate situation.

Example: The experiment has broader implications for our understanding of memory.

long-term implications

Meaning: effects that may appear or continue far into the future.

Example: Scientists are still studying the long-term implications of climate change.

practical implications

Meaning: real-world effects or uses.

Example: The theory has practical implications for classroom teaching.

policy implications

Meaning: effects on government rules, decisions, or public programs.

Example: The research has clear policy implications for urban planning.

economic implications

Meaning: effects related to money, jobs, markets, or production.

Example: Automation has economic implications for both workers and employers.

social implications

Meaning: effects on people, communities, or relationships.

Example: The spread of remote work has social implications for city life.

environmental implications

Meaning: effects on nature, ecosystems, or climate.

Example: The dam project has environmental implications for nearby wetlands.

implications for

Meaning: effects on a particular field, group, or situation.

Example: The discovery has implications for future space exploration.

the implication is that

Meaning: the indirect meaning or suggested conclusion is that something is true.

Example: The implication is that students need more support outside the classroom.

by implication

Meaning: as an indirect result of what has been said or shown.

Example: By implication, the study questions several older theories.

consider the implications

Meaning: think carefully about possible results.

Example: Researchers must consider the implications of using personal data.

implication in a scandal

Meaning: suggested involvement in a public wrongdoing.

Example: The official denied any implication in the scandal.

Direct Synonyms & Antonyms

Direct synonyms

consequence effect suggestion involvement

For the “result” meaning, consequence and effect are closest. For the “indirect meaning” sense, suggestion or implied meaning is closer. For the negative legal or political sense, involvement is the closest simple synonym.

Direct antonyms

explicit statement direct statement irrelevance disconnection

Implication has no perfect single-word antonym in all meanings. For indirect meaning, the opposite is an explicit statement. For connection to wrongdoing, the opposite idea is disconnection or lack of involvement.

Etymology

Implication comes from Latin roots connected to the idea of “folding into” or “entangling.” This history helps explain the modern meanings: an implication is a meaning folded into a statement, a consequence folded into an action, or a person’s connection to a situation.

Practice Exercises

1. Word family choice

Choose the best member of the word family for each blank.

  1. The speaker did not say the policy had failed, but she clearly __________ it.
  2. The discovery has several important __________ for medical research.
  3. The agreement contained an __________ promise to share information.

2. Correct or incorrect?

Decide whether each sentence uses the word correctly. If it is wrong, correct it.

  1. The new technology may have serious implications for employment.
  2. The author implications that the theory is incomplete.
  3. The official denied his implication in the corruption scandal.

3. Collocation practice

Complete each sentence with a natural collocation.

  1. The study has __________ implications for how schools teach reading.
  2. Scientists are examining the __________ implications of the new energy project.
  3. The __________ is that the original explanation was too simple.
Answer Key
  1. implied — The verb form is needed: “she clearly implied it.”
  2. implications — The plural noun is natural with “several important.”
  3. implicit — The adjective means “not directly stated.”
  4. Correct. “Implications for employment” is a natural TOEFL-style collocation.
  5. Incorrect. Correct form: “The author implies that the theory is incomplete.”
  6. Correct. “Implication in a scandal” means suggested involvement in wrongdoing.
  7. important or major — Both are natural: “important/major implications.”
  8. environmental — This fits “energy project.”
  9. implication — Pattern: “The implication is that + clause.”

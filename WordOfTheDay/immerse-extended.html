Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

Put completely under liquid

transitive verb /ɪˈmɝːs/ Common in scientific, technical, instructional, and formal English.

to put something completely below the surface of a liquid

In simpler words: put completely under liquid

Typical pattern: immerse + object + in + liquid

Natural examples

  1. The technician immersed the metal sample in a chemical solution.
  2. Before planting, immerse the seeds in warm water for several hours.
  3. The researcher carefully immersed the instrument in the tank.
  4. Do not immerse the electrical device in water.
  5. The cloth was immersed in dye until it developed a deep blue color.
  6. Scientists immersed the sensors in seawater to test their durability.
  7. The container must remain tightly closed when it is immersed in the liquid.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The object goes completely below the surface rather than only touching the liquid.

Usual implication: The action is often deliberate and done for testing, cleaning, treating, coloring, or preparing something.

Compared with “dip”: Dip usually means putting something into a liquid briefly or only partly. Immerse normally means putting it completely below the surface.

Grammar notes

How to use it
This verb normally needs an object. Say immerse the cloth in water, not simply immerse in water.
What can follow it
Put the object directly after immerse, and then use in before the liquid.
Common preposition
Use immerse something in a liquid. For example: Immerse the sample in water.
Position in a sentence
In passive sentences, the object comes first: The sample was immersed in water.

Useful patterns

immerse + object + in + liquid

Use this pattern when someone puts an object completely below the surface of a liquid.

Example: The scientist immersed the material in a salt solution.

be immersed in + liquid

Use this pattern when the focus is on the object under the liquid rather than the person performing the action.

Example: The roots should be immersed in water before planting.

fully or completely immerse + object

Use fully or completely to emphasize that no part should remain above the surface.

Example: Fully immerse the fabric to ensure an even color.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: Immerse the cloth into the solution for ten minutes.

Correct: Immerse the cloth in the solution for ten minutes.

Use in after immerse when naming the liquid.

Wrong: The scientist immersed in water the sample.

Correct: The scientist immersed the sample in water.

Put the object directly after immerse, followed by in and the liquid.

Wrong: The device should not immerse in water.

Correct: The device should not be immersed in water.

Use be immersed when the device receives the action.

Meaning 2

Become deeply involved

transitive verb /ɪˈmɝːs/ Common in academic, educational, professional, and everyday English.

to involve yourself or another person deeply in an activity, subject, culture, or experience so that it receives most or all of the person's attention

In simpler words: become completely involved in something

Typical pattern: immerse yourself in + noun; be immersed in + noun; immerse someone in + noun

Natural examples

  1. Living with a host family allowed her to immerse herself in the local culture.
  2. He immersed himself in his research and rarely took a day off.
  3. Students learn more quickly when they are immersed in an English-speaking environment.
  4. The course immerses participants in real-world problems from the first week.
  5. She spent the summer immersing herself in the history of the region.
  6. Readers can easily become immersed in the novel's detailed world.
  7. The internship gave students a chance to immerse themselves in professional laboratory work.
  8. After the move, the family made an effort to immerse themselves in community life.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The person gives strong, sustained attention to an activity or becomes surrounded by a new environment or experience.

Usual implication: This deep involvement often helps the person learn, understand, experience, or enjoy something more fully.

Compared with “participate”: Participate means take part in an activity. Immerse yourself in something suggests much deeper involvement and attention.

Grammar notes

How to use it
When the subject chooses to become deeply involved, use immerse yourself in something. Change yourself to myself, himself, herself, ourselves, or themselves when needed.
What can follow it
It is followed by in and then a noun or an -ing form, such as immerse yourself in a culture or in studying a language.
Common preposition
Use in, not into or with: immerse yourself in the subject.
Position in a sentence
The passive form be immersed in is also very common: She was immersed in her work.

Useful patterns

immerse yourself in + noun or -ing form

Use this pattern when someone chooses to become deeply involved in something.

Example: To improve quickly, immerse yourself in speaking and listening to English every day.

be immersed in + activity or experience

Use this pattern when someone is giving something their full attention or is completely surrounded by an experience.

Example: The students were immersed in a discussion of climate policy.

immerse someone in + environment or subject

Use this pattern when a course, program, or experience gives someone intense exposure to something.

Example: The program immerses students in the daily life of the community.

become deeply or fully immersed in + noun

Use this pattern to emphasize increasing or complete involvement.

Example: She became deeply immersed in the study of ancient languages.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: He immersed in his studies for several weeks.

Correct: He immersed himself in his studies for several weeks.

When the subject actively becomes involved, use immerse himself, herself, yourself, or another matching form.

Wrong: She immersed herself into the local culture.

Correct: She immersed herself in the local culture.

Use in after immerse yourself.

Wrong: The students were immersed with the project.

Correct: The students were immersed in the project.

Use immersed in, not immersed with.

Wrong: The course immerses to students in practical research.

Correct: The course immerses students in practical research.

Put the person directly after immerse. Do not use to.

Morphology

Word family

Only common and educationally useful family members are included.

countable and uncountable noun

immersion

/ɪˈmɝːʒən/

the act of putting something under a liquid, or the experience of being deeply involved in a subject, activity, language, or culture

Relationship: Immersion is the noun form of immerse.

Pattern: immersion in + liquid, subject, activity, language, or culture

Usage note: It is often uncountable. A countable use is possible for a particular intensive learning experience, such as a two-week immersion.

  1. The samples were tested after immersion in salt water.
  2. Language immersion can help learners develop more natural listening skills.
  3. Her complete immersion in the project led to several important discoveries.

adjective

immersive

/ɪˈmɝːsɪv/

creating a strong feeling of being completely surrounded by and involved in an experience

Relationship: Immersive describes an experience or environment that makes people feel deeply involved.

Pattern: immersive + experience, environment, program, technology, or exhibition

Usage note: This adjective is especially common in education, technology, games, art, and entertainment.

  1. The museum uses sound and projected images to create an immersive experience.
  2. Students participated in an immersive language program.
  3. Virtual reality can provide an immersive learning environment.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

submerge

transitive and intransitive verb · Put completely under liquid

Shared meaning: Both words can mean put or go completely below the surface of a liquid.

Difference: Submerge also means go or put something completely below a liquid's surface. Immerse more often suggests a deliberate action for a purpose such as testing, washing, or treatment, while submerge is also common for objects that sink or become covered by water.

Example: Floodwater submerged several roads near the river.

dip

transitive and intransitive verb · Put completely under liquid

Shared meaning: Both words describe putting something into a liquid.

Difference: Dip usually means put something into a liquid briefly or only partly. Immerse normally means put it completely under the surface, often for longer.

Example: She dipped the brush into the paint.

be absorbed in

verb phrase · Become deeply involved

Shared meaning: Both expressions describe being deeply involved in an activity or subject.

Difference: Be absorbed in focuses on giving something your complete attention. Immerse yourself in can also suggest entering a wider environment, culture, or experience.

Example: She was absorbed in a detailed study of medieval history.

engross

transitive verb · Become deeply involved

Shared meaning: Both words involve complete or nearly complete attention.

Difference: Engross means hold someone's complete attention. Immerse can describe either choosing to become deeply involved or being placed in an environment that encourages deep involvement.

Example: The documentary engrossed viewers from the opening scene.

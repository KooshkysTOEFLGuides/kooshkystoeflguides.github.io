Quick orientation

Usage overview

Core idea

The word refers to something held in the mind, either as an idea or as a degree of understanding.

Register

Neutral to formal. It is common in academic discussion, especially when describing beliefs that may be questioned.

Where it appears

Learners often meet notion in lectures, essays, and discussions about theories, public beliefs, and incomplete knowledge.

TOEFL value

It is useful for recognizing and paraphrasing claims, assumptions, and levels of understanding in academic passages and discussions.

Sound

Pronunciation

noun

/ˈnoʊʃən/

The pronunciation is the same in all common meanings.

Dictionary guide

Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

an idea or belief

countable noun /ˈnoʊʃən/ neutral to formal

An idea, belief, or opinion that someone has, especially one that may not be fully supported by evidence.

In simpler words: an idea that someone believes

Typical pattern: the notion that + clause

Common contexts

scientific theoriessocial beliefshistorical interpretationspublic opinion

Natural examples

  1. The experiment weakened the notion that the two species had evolved independently.
  2. Many historians reject the notion that the conflict had a single cause.
  3. The professor challenged the common notion that creativity cannot be taught.
  4. Her proposal is based on the notion that smaller classes improve participation.
  5. The article questions the traditional notion of economic progress.
  6. There is a widespread notion that older buildings are always less energy-efficient.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: Notion often presents an idea as something people hold in their minds rather than as an established fact.

Usual implication: It can suggest that the idea is general, simplified, uncertain, or open to challenge, although it is not automatically false.

Compared with “fact”: A fact is supported as true, while a notion is an idea or belief that may still require evidence.

Grammar notes

Countability
This meaning is countable. Use a notion, the notion, or notions. It is often singular when followed by that or of.
Transitivity
Not applicable because this sense is not a verb.
Complements and objects
It commonly takes a that-clause or an of-phrase: the notion that prices will fall; the notion of unlimited growth.
Prepositions
Use notion of before a noun or gerund. A that-clause follows notion directly without a preposition.
Form and position
It functions as the head of a noun phrase and may be modified by adjectives such as common, traditional, false, or preconceived.

Useful patterns

the notion that + clause

Introduces the content of an idea or belief.

Example: The study disputes the notion that all migration is caused by climate.

the notion of + noun or gerund

Names the subject or concept contained in the idea.

Example: The notion of sharing resources was unfamiliar to the isolated community.

challenge or reject the notion

Shows that a speaker or source disagrees with a belief.

Example: Recent evidence challenges the notion that the settlement was temporary.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The researcher rejected the notion about that the process was random.

Correct: The researcher rejected the notion that the process was random.

Do not place about before a that-clause after notion.

Wrong: The evidence proved the notion as a fact.

Correct: The evidence supported the notion.

Support a notion is natural, but prove a notion as a fact is an awkward and misleading combination.

TOEFL use

Reading and listening

In academic input, notion often introduces a belief that a professor, author, or study later supports, qualifies, or challenges.

Writing and speaking

It can help present and evaluate a general belief, especially in patterns such as the notion that and challenge the notion.

Paraphrase tip

Depending on context, notion may be paraphrased as idea, belief, assumption, or view, but the surrounding evidence determines the closest choice.

Meaning 2

a basic or limited understanding

countable noun, usually singular /ˈnoʊʃən/ neutral to formal

A general or incomplete understanding of something rather than detailed knowledge.

In simpler words: a rough understanding

Typical pattern: have some or no notion of + noun

Common contexts

limited knowledgetechnical subjectspast conditionsother people’s experiences

Natural examples

  1. Most visitors have only a vague notion of how the water system operates.
  2. The early settlers had little notion of the difficulties they would face.
  3. She has a clear notion of the project’s main goal but not its technical details.
  4. The students had no notion of how much evidence the assignment required.
  5. Before the lecture, I had only a general notion of plate movement.
  6. People who have never farmed may have little notion of how strongly rainfall affects crop yields.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: This use focuses on the amount or clarity of a person’s understanding.

Usual implication: It often suggests that the knowledge is broad, rough, or incomplete rather than precise and expert.

Compared with “knowledge”: Knowledge can be detailed and well-supported, while a notion in this sense is usually a general or partial understanding.

Grammar notes

Countability
This use is countable but is usually singular after have: a notion, some notion, little notion, or no notion.
Transitivity
Not applicable because this sense is not a verb.
Complements and objects
It commonly follows have and takes an of-phrase or a wh-clause: have no notion of the cost; have no notion how the device works.
Prepositions
Use of before a noun or gerund. In natural English, a wh-clause may follow notion without of.
Form and position
Adjectives and determiners often show the degree of understanding: a vague notion, some notion, little notion, no notion.

Useful patterns

have a vague or general notion of + noun

Shows a rough but incomplete understanding.

Example: The audience had a general notion of the theory before the speaker explained the evidence.

have little or no notion of + noun

Shows very limited understanding.

Example: The planners had little notion of the region’s seasonal water shortage.

have no notion how or why + clause

Shows that someone does not understand a process or reason.

Example: Researchers still have no notion why the behavior appears only in winter.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: I have a notion about how the machine works, but only generally.

Correct: I have a general notion of how the machine works.

For a degree of understanding, notion of is more natural than notion about.

Wrong: She has many notions of the procedure.

Correct: She has a clear notion of the procedure.

This sense normally describes the degree of one person’s understanding, so a singular noun with a degree adjective is more natural.

TOEFL use

Reading and listening

This sense may describe how much a group understood before a discovery, lecture, or technological development.

Writing and speaking

It is useful for carefully describing partial knowledge, especially with vague, general, little, or no.

Paraphrase tip

In this sense, notion is close to understanding or idea of how something works, not to a strongly held belief.

Morphology

Word family

Only common and educationally useful family members are included.

adjective

notional

/ˈnoʊʃənəl/

Existing mainly as an idea, estimate, or formal value rather than as something fully real or fixed.

Relationship: It describes something based on a notion, assumed value, or theoretical amount.

Pattern: notional + noun

Usage note: This adjective is more formal and less common than notion. It often appears in economics, planning, and technical discussion.

  1. The report uses a notional cost to compare the two plans.
  2. The border was notional because no physical barrier marked it.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

idea

countable noun · an idea or belief

Shared meaning: Both words can refer to something formed in the mind.

Difference: Idea is broader and more neutral. Notion often sounds more formal and may suggest an untested or simplified belief.

Example: The idea that forests recover immediately after fire is misleading.

belief

countable noun · an idea or belief

Shared meaning: Both can describe something a person accepts as true.

Difference: Belief emphasizes acceptance or conviction, while notion can be weaker and more open to doubt.

Example: The community maintained a strong belief in the medicinal value of the plant.

understanding

countable or uncountable noun · a basic or limited understanding

Shared meaning: Both can refer to what someone knows about a subject.

Difference: Understanding is broader and can be deep or complete; notion usually suggests a general or incomplete grasp.

Example: The course gave students a deeper understanding of climate feedback systems.

Precision

Words learners may confuse

concept

countable noun

Why it is confusing: Both words can refer to an idea in the mind and often appear in academic contexts.

Key difference: Concept usually names an organized or defined idea, while notion often refers to a person’s belief or rough understanding.

notion: The new evidence undermined the notion that the culture had remained isolated.

concept: Natural selection is a central concept in evolutionary biology.

Word history

Etymology

Only confidently documented details are shown.

Origin

English received the word through French from Latin.

Historical form

It is related to Latin notio, a word connected with becoming acquainted with or forming an understanding of something.

Early meaning

Earlier uses were connected with knowledge, understanding, and an idea held in the mind.

Development

The modern meanings developed around two related senses: an idea or belief, and a degree of understanding.

Memory link

Think of a notion as something known or formed in the mind, whether it is a belief or only a rough understanding.

Accuracy note

The historical connection can support memory, but it does not mean that notion and knowledge are interchangeable in modern English.

Word family choice

1. notional
The committee used a notional budget to compare several possible designs.
The blank modifies budget, so the adjective notional is required.

2. notion
The lecture challenged the notion that deserts contain very little biological diversity.
The countable noun notion names the belief being challenged.

3. notional
The boundary was largely notional because no fence or natural feature marked it.
Notional describes something that exists mainly as an idea rather than as a physical reality.

Correct or incorrect?

1. Correct
No correction needed.
The pattern challenge the notion that plus a clause is natural and accurate.

2. Incorrect
The students had only a vague notion of how the instrument worked.
When notion means a degree of understanding, a singular noun with a degree expression is more natural.

3. Incorrect
The author rejected the notion that technology alone caused the change.
A that-clause follows notion directly; about is not used before that.

Collocation practice

1. challenged
The discovery challenged the notion that the settlement had been abandoned after one season.
Challenge the notion is a common academic collocation for presenting conflicting evidence.

2. preconceived
The survey revealed several preconceived notions about the abilities of older workers.
Preconceived notion means an opinion formed before enough evidence is available.

3. vague
Before the lab, we had only a vague notion of how the reaction would proceed.
A vague notion is an unclear or incomplete understanding.

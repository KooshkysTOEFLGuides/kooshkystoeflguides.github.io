Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

Based on personal judgment

adjective /səbˈdʒɛktɪv/ neutral to formal; common in academic, professional, artistic, and everyday discussions
🔊 Hear adjective pronunciation

based on a person's opinions, feelings, or preferences rather than only on facts that can be measured or proved

In simpler words: based on personal opinion

Typical pattern: subjective + judgment, opinion, assessment, interpretation, or criterion

Natural examples

  1. Judgments about artistic quality are often highly subjective.
  2. The interview process was criticized because the final decisions seemed too subjective.
  3. Whether a building is beautiful is partly a subjective question.
  4. The researcher tried to separate subjective impressions from measurable evidence.
  5. Teachers should use clear criteria to make essay grading less subjective.
  6. Her review provides a subjective interpretation of the film rather than a neutral summary.
  7. Ideas about the ideal work environment are subjective and may vary considerably from person to person.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The word emphasizes that a judgment is influenced by the person making it.

Usual implication: A subjective judgment is not automatically unreasonable or wrong, but different people may reach different conclusions.

Compared with “objective”: Subjective judgments depend on personal views or feelings. Objective judgments are based as much as possible on observable facts and agreed standards.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it before a noun, as in a subjective assessment, or after a linking verb, as in The assessment is subjective.
What can follow it
It commonly appears before nouns such as judgment, opinion, view, assessment, interpretation, impression, and criterion.
Position in a sentence
Words such as highly, largely, partly, inherently, and necessarily often come before subjective.

Useful patterns

a subjective judgment or assessment

Use this for a decision influenced by the evaluator's personal views.

Example: The selection process depends partly on a subjective assessment of leadership potential.

be highly or largely subjective

Use this when personal opinion strongly affects a judgment.

Example: The choice of the best design is highly subjective.

a subjective interpretation of + noun

Use this for an explanation shaped by one person's perspective.

Example: The article offers a subjective interpretation of the historical evidence.

make something less subjective

Use this when rules or measurements reduce the influence of personal opinion.

Example: A detailed scoring guide can make the evaluation less subjective.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The decision was subjective from the manager's personal opinion.

Correct: The decision was based on the manager's subjective opinion.

Use subjective directly before the noun it describes, or say that something is based on a subjective judgment or opinion.

Wrong: Subjective always means unfair or incorrect.

Correct: Subjective means influenced by personal opinion or feeling, but it does not always mean unfair or incorrect.

A judgment can be subjective without being dishonest or unreasonable.

Wrong: The results were very subjectively.

Correct: The results were very subjective.

Use the adjective subjective after were. Subjectively is an adverb.

Meaning 2

Existing in personal experience

adjective /səbˈdʒɛktɪv/ formal; common in psychology, medicine, sociology, philosophy, and research writing
🔊 Hear adjective pronunciation

experienced, perceived, or understood within an individual person's mind and therefore not fully observable or measurable by others

In simpler words: personally experienced or perceived

Typical pattern: subjective + experience, perception, feeling, report, rating, or well-being

Natural examples

  1. Pain is a subjective experience that can be difficult to measure precisely.
  2. The survey asked participants to report their subjective sense of well-being.
  3. People may give different descriptions of the same event because perception is subjective.
  4. The study combined biological measurements with patients' subjective reports of fatigue.
  5. Time can feel slower or faster depending on a person's subjective experience.
  6. The taste of the medicine was evaluated through subjective ratings provided by volunteers.
  7. Researchers distinguished objective sleep duration from subjective sleep quality.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: This sense focuses on what a person experiences internally, such as pain, satisfaction, taste, or perceived quality.

Usual implication: The experience may be real and important even when another person cannot directly observe or measure it.

Compared with “objective”: A subjective measure records a person's own experience or report. An objective measure records something observable or measurable independently, such as temperature, distance, or heart rate.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it before a noun naming an internal experience, perception, or self-reported measure.
What can follow it
Common nouns include experience, perception, feeling, report, rating, well-being, awareness, and quality.
Position in a sentence
In research writing, subjective is often contrasted directly with objective.

Useful patterns

subjective experience of + noun

Use this for how an event or condition feels to an individual.

Example: The interviews explored each patient's subjective experience of recovery.

subjective report or rating

Use this for information supplied by a person about their own experience.

Example: The researchers collected subjective ratings of stress after each task.

subjective sense of + noun

Use this for a personally felt state or impression.

Example: Regular social contact improved participants' subjective sense of belonging.

subjective and objective measures

Use this when comparing self-reported experience with independently observable data.

Example: The study used both subjective and objective measures of sleep quality.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: Because pain is subjective, it is not real.

Correct: Pain is subjective because it is experienced personally, but it is still real.

Subjective means internally experienced, not imaginary or unimportant.

Wrong: The study measured subjectively well-being.

Correct: The study measured subjective well-being.

Use the adjective subjective before the noun well-being.

Wrong: The patient's subjective was recorded in the survey.

Correct: The patient's subjective experience was recorded in the survey.

Subjective is an adjective and normally needs a noun after it in this position.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

personal

adjective · all senses

Shared meaning: Both can refer to an individual's own views or experience.

Difference: Personal is broader and simply means belonging or relating to an individual. Subjective specifically emphasizes that a judgment or experience depends on that individual's perspective.

Example: Her response reflected her personal preferences.

interpretive

adjective · Based on personal judgment

Shared meaning: Both can describe conclusions shaped by a person's understanding.

Difference: Interpretive describes an explanation based on how evidence or meaning is understood. Subjective more broadly emphasizes personal opinion or feeling.

Example: The exhibition includes an interpretive essay about the artist's work.

self-reported

adjective · Existing in personal experience

Shared meaning: Both may refer to information based on a person's own account.

Difference: Self-reported specifically means supplied by the person being studied. Subjective is broader and describes the personal nature of the experience or judgment itself.

Example: The study compared self-reported exercise with data from activity monitors.

Direct or useful antonyms

objective

adjective · all senses

Opposition: Objective judgments and measurements rely on observable facts or agreed standards, while subjective ones depend on personal opinion, perception, or experience.

Usage note: In many real situations, an evaluation can contain both subjective and objective elements.

Example: The researchers used objective measurements of blood pressure.

Precision

Words learners may confuse

biased

adjective

Why it is confusing: Both words may describe judgments that are not completely neutral.

Key difference: Subjective means influenced by personal perspective. Biased means unfairly favoring or opposing a person, group, or idea. A judgment can be subjective without being biased.

subjective: The rating is subjective because it depends on personal taste.

biased: The report was biased because it ignored evidence that opposed the author's position.

subject to

adjective versus adjective phrase

Why it is confusing: The forms begin with the same letters but have unrelated uses.

Key difference: Subjective is an adjective meaning based on personal opinion or experience. Subject to is a separate expression meaning affected by, dependent on, or required to follow something.

subjective: The choice of the best painting is subjective.

subject to: The schedule is subject to change.

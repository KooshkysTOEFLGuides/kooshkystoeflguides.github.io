Meanings and usage

Meaning 1

Chosen representative

countable noun /ˈdɛləɡət/ neutral to formal; common in politics, conferences, unions, institutions, and international organizations
🔊 Hear countable noun pronunciation

Pronunciation: /ˈdɛləɡət/ in the phrase “a delegate.” This word has another pronunciation in at least one other meaning.

a person chosen or elected to represent a group at a meeting, conference, or organization

In simpler words: a person who represents a group

Typical pattern: a delegate to + event or a delegate from + group or place

Natural examples

  1. Each country sent several delegates to the international conference.
  2. The union delegates voted in favor of the proposed agreement.
  3. She attended the convention as a delegate from her university.
  4. Delegates from more than fifty nations participated in the climate summit.
  5. The committee elected two student delegates to present its concerns.
  6. Conference delegates received copies of the final report.
  7. The delegate spoke on behalf of workers in the manufacturing sector.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: A delegate does not attend only as a private individual; the person represents the interests or views of a larger group.

Usual implication: The delegate may be expected to speak, negotiate, or vote on behalf of the group.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use a delegate for one representative and delegates for more than one.
What can follow it
Use to before the meeting or organization attended, and from before the place or group represented.
Common preposition
Say a delegate to the conference but a delegate from the university.
Noun use
This is a countable noun, so the singular normally needs a, an, or the.

Useful patterns

a delegate to + conference or meeting

Use this to identify the event or organization that the person attends as a representative.

Example: He was selected as a delegate to the national convention.

a delegate from + country, institution, or group

Use this to identify the people or place that the delegate represents.

Example: A delegate from the teachers' union addressed the committee.

send or elect a delegate

Use this when a group chooses someone to represent it.

Example: Each department elected a delegate to the university council.

conference delegates

Use this for the people officially attending a conference as representatives.

Example: Conference delegates discussed several proposals on the final day.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: She attended the meeting as delegate.

Correct: She attended the meeting as a delegate.

The singular countable noun normally needs a.

Wrong: He was a delegate of the international conference.

Correct: He was a delegate to the international conference.

Use delegate to for the event attended.

Wrong: She was a delegate to her university.

Correct: She was a delegate from her university.

Use delegate from for the group or institution represented.

Meaning 2

Give someone a task

transitive verb /ˈdɛləˌɡeɪt/ neutral to formal; common in business, management, education, and professional contexts
🔊 Hear transitive verb pronunciation

Pronunciation: /ˈdɛləˌɡeɪt/ in the phrase “delegate tasks.” This word has another pronunciation in at least one other meaning.

to give a task or responsibility to another person so that they do it on your behalf

In simpler words: give part of your work to someone else

Typical pattern: delegate + task or responsibility + to + person

Natural examples

  1. Effective managers know when to delegate routine tasks to other team members.
  2. The professor delegated responsibility for collecting the data to two research assistants.
  3. She delegated the preparation of the monthly report to her deputy.
  4. The team leader delegated each stage of the project to a different specialist.
  5. You should delegate some of your work instead of trying to do everything yourself.
  6. The director was reluctant to delegate important responsibilities.
  7. By delegating administrative tasks, doctors can spend more time with patients.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: Delegating means assigning work to another person while usually remaining responsible for the overall result.

Usual implication: Good delegation involves choosing a suitable person and giving them enough information or authority to complete the task.

Compared with “assign”: Assign is a general word for giving someone a task. Delegate often suggests that the task originally belonged to a person with greater responsibility or authority.

Grammar notes

How to use it
This verb normally needs the task or responsibility after it.
What can follow it
It is often followed by task, duty, responsibility, work, decision, or the name of an activity.
Common preposition
Use to before the person receiving the task, as in delegate the work to an assistant.
Position in a sentence
The task normally comes before to and the person: delegate a task to someone.

Useful patterns

delegate + task or duty + to + person

Use this when giving a particular piece of work to someone else.

Example: The manager delegated the task to an experienced employee.

delegate responsibility for + noun or -ing form + to + person

Use this when another person becomes responsible for a particular activity.

Example: She delegated responsibility for organizing the event to her assistant.

delegate the preparation or management of + noun

Use this for formal descriptions of work given to another person.

Example: The director delegated the management of the project to a senior engineer.

learn or be willing to delegate

Use this without an object when the type of work is already clear.

Example: Successful leaders must learn to delegate.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The manager delegated to Sara the report.

Correct: The manager delegated the report to Sara.

The usual order is delegate the task to the person.

Wrong: She delegated her assistant to prepare the report.

Correct: She delegated the preparation of the report to her assistant.

It is usually more natural to delegate a task or responsibility to someone.

Wrong: He delegated about organizing the conference.

Correct: He delegated responsibility for organizing the conference.

Do not use about after delegate. Name the task or responsibility directly.

Meaning 3

Transfer authority

transitive verb /ˈdɛləˌɡeɪt/ formal; common in law, government, administration, and organizational management
🔊 Hear transitive verb pronunciation

Pronunciation: /ˈdɛləˌɡeɪt/ in the phrase “delegate authority.” This word has another pronunciation in at least one other meaning.

to officially give part of your authority or decision-making power to another person or organization

In simpler words: officially give someone part of your power

Typical pattern: delegate + authority, power, control, or responsibility + to + person or organization

Natural examples

  1. The director delegated authority to approve small purchases to department managers.
  2. National governments sometimes delegate certain powers to regional authorities.
  3. The law allows the agency to delegate some of its responsibilities to local councils.
  4. Senior officials may delegate decision-making authority to local administrators.
  5. The board delegated control of daily operations to the executive director.
  6. Parliament can delegate limited regulatory powers to government agencies.
  7. The president delegated responsibility for the negotiations to the foreign minister.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: This meaning concerns official power rather than merely the performance of a task.

Usual implication: The original authority often keeps overall responsibility and may place limits on the power being transferred.

Compared with “surrender”: To surrender authority is to give it up, often completely. To delegate authority is usually to transfer only part of it while keeping overall control or responsibility.

Grammar notes

How to use it
This verb normally needs the authority, power, control, or responsibility being transferred after it.
What can follow it
Common objects include authority, power, control, responsibility, decision-making, and functions.
Common preposition
Use to before the person, department, or organization receiving the authority.
Position in a sentence
The usual order is delegate authority to someone.

Useful patterns

delegate authority to + person or organization

Use this when someone officially gives another party the power to make certain decisions.

Example: The minister delegated authority to regional officials.

delegate power over + area + to + body

Use this to identify both the area of authority and the organization receiving it.

Example: The law delegates power over local transportation to municipal governments.

delegate decision-making responsibility

Use this when another person or group is allowed to make particular choices.

Example: The company delegated decision-making responsibility to local branches.

be delegated to + person or organization

Use the passive form when the transferred authority is the main focus.

Example: Certain administrative powers were delegated to the committee.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The board delegated authority on the manager.

Correct: The board delegated authority to the manager.

Use to for the person or organization receiving authority.

Wrong: The government delegated the local council with new powers.

Correct: The government delegated new powers to the local council.

The usual pattern is delegate something to someone.

Wrong: The director delegated all responsibility and was no longer accountable.

Correct: The director delegated several responsibilities but remained accountable for the final results.

Delegating work or authority does not necessarily remove the original person's overall responsibility.

a group of people chosen to represent others, or the act of giving tasks or authority to another person

Relationship: This noun can refer either to a group of delegates or to the process of delegating work or authority.

Pattern: a delegation from + place, a delegation of + representatives, or the delegation of + task or authority

Usage note: Use a delegation for a representative group. Delegation is often uncountable when it means the management process.

  2. Effective delegation allows managers to focus on their most important responsibilities.
  3. The international delegation visited several research institutions.
  4. Poor delegation can leave employees confused about their responsibilities.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

representative

countable noun · Chosen representative

Shared meaning: Both words describe someone who acts on behalf of a larger group.

Difference: Representative is a broad term for anyone who acts or speaks for others. Delegate often refers specifically to someone formally chosen to attend a meeting, conference, or organization.

Example: Each organization sent a representative to the meeting.

assign

transitive verb · Give someone a task

Shared meaning: Both verbs can mean giving work to another person.

Difference: Assign simply means give someone a task. Delegate often suggests that a person passes on work that originally belonged to them while keeping overall responsibility.

Example: The professor assigned each student a separate research topic.

entrust

transitive verb · Give someone a task

Shared meaning: Both verbs can describe giving another person responsibility.

Difference: Entrust emphasizes confidence that someone will take care of an important task, object, or person. Delegate focuses on transferring responsibility for work.

Example: The director entrusted her assistant with the confidential documents.

authorize

transitive verb · Transfer authority

Shared meaning: Both verbs can involve officially giving someone power to act.

Difference: Authorize means officially give permission to do something. Delegate means transfer some of your own authority or responsibility to another party.

Example: The committee authorized the director to sign the agreement.

Precision

Words learners may confuse

designate

transitive verb

Why it is confusing: Both formal verbs can involve giving someone a role, but they describe different actions.

Key difference: Delegate means give someone a task or authority. Designate usually means officially choose, name, or identify someone or something for a role or purpose.

delegate: The director delegated responsibility for the project to Maria.

designate: The director designated Maria as the project leader.

Dictionary guide

Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

Too satisfied to notice risks

adjective /kəmˈpleɪsənt/ neutral to formal; common in academic, professional, political, health, and safety discussions
🔊 Hear adjective pronunciation

too satisfied with a situation or with your own abilities, so that you do not notice problems, make improvements, or prepare for possible danger

In simpler words: too comfortable and no longer careful

Typical pattern: become or grow complacent about + problem, risk, or situation

Natural examples

  1. After several successful years, the company became complacent and stopped investing in innovation.
  2. Public health officials warned people not to become complacent about the disease.
  3. The team grew complacent after taking an early lead and eventually lost the game.
  4. Students should not become complacent simply because they performed well on one examination.
  5. The report argues that economic growth made policymakers complacent about rising inequality.
  6. Strong sales may create a complacent attitude toward changes in consumer behavior.
  7. The absence of recent accidents should not make workers complacent about safety.
  8. Competitors quickly gained ground while the market leader remained complacent.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: Complacent does not simply mean calm, comfortable, or satisfied. It means that satisfaction has become excessive and has reduced attention, effort, or caution.

Usual implication: The word is negative and usually suggests that the person or group may fail, fall behind, or face preventable problems.

Compared with “content”: Content means peacefully satisfied and is usually positive or neutral. Complacent means too satisfied to recognize risks or continue improving and is negative.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it after a linking verb such as be, become, grow, seem, or remain, or before a noun such as attitude or leadership.
What can follow it
It is often followed by about and then a problem, risk, achievement, or situation.
Common preposition
Use complacent about something, as in complacent about safety. Do not normally use complacent of or complacent with.
Position in a sentence
It commonly appears after become or grow when describing a change in attitude.

Useful patterns

become or grow complacent

Use this when someone gradually becomes less careful or ambitious because they feel too secure.

Example: The organization grew complacent after years without serious competition.

be complacent about + noun

Use this to identify the problem, risk, or situation that someone is not taking seriously enough.

Example: We cannot afford to be complacent about cybersecurity.

be complacent about + -ing form

Use this when describing an activity or development that someone is treating with too little concern.

Example: Officials were complacent about allowing the infrastructure to deteriorate.

a complacent attitude

Use this for a way of thinking that ignores danger or the need for improvement.

Example: A complacent attitude toward workplace safety can have serious consequences.

remain complacent despite + noun

Use this when someone continues to feel too secure even though warning signs exist.

Example: The company remained complacent despite a steady decline in customer satisfaction.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The students were complacent with the coming examination.

Correct: The students were complacent about the coming examination.

Use complacent about to identify the matter that someone is not taking seriously enough.

Wrong: She felt complacent because she had completed a difficult project and deserved to relax.

Correct: She felt satisfied because she had completed a difficult project and deserved to relax.

Complacent is negative and suggests a harmful lack of effort or caution. Use satisfied when the feeling is reasonable.

Wrong: The manager complacented after the company became successful.

Correct: The manager became complacent after the company became successful.

Complacent is an adjective, not a verb. Use become complacent.

Wrong: The government should not be complacent of the growing risk.

Correct: The government should not be complacent about the growing risk.

Use about, not of, after complacent.

Morphology

Word family

Only common and educationally useful family members are included.

uncountable noun

complacency

/kəmˈpleɪsənsi/

a feeling of excessive satisfaction that causes someone to ignore problems, risks, or the need for improvement

Relationship: This noun names the harmful attitude or state described by complacent.

Pattern: complacency about + issue or a sense of complacency

Usage note: It is normally uncountable, so do not usually use a or an before it.

  1. The recent improvement should not lead to complacency.
  2. Officials warned that public complacency could allow the disease to spread again.
  3. The company's decline was partly caused by years of managerial complacency.

adverb

complacently

/kəmˈpleɪsəntli/

in a way that shows excessive satisfaction and too little awareness of problems or risks

Relationship: This adverb describes an action performed with a complacent attitude.

Pattern: complacently + assume, believe, ignore, or observe

Usage note: It is less common than complacent and complacency.

  1. The company complacently assumed that customers would remain loyal.
  2. Officials watched complacently as the warning signs became more serious.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

self-satisfied

adjective · Too satisfied to notice risks

Shared meaning: Both describe an excessive and usually negative form of satisfaction.

Difference: Self-satisfied emphasizes excessive satisfaction with yourself or your achievements. Complacent also emphasizes the resulting failure to notice risks or continue improving.

Example: His self-satisfied expression annoyed the other members of the team.

smug

adjective · Too satisfied to notice risks

Shared meaning: Both can describe someone who is excessively pleased with themselves.

Difference: Smug strongly suggests that someone feels superior and often shows it in an irritating way. Complacent focuses more on reduced caution, effort, or awareness.

Example: She sounded smug after her prediction proved correct.

overconfident

adjective · Too satisfied to notice risks

Shared meaning: Both attitudes can cause someone to underestimate difficulties or risks.

Difference: Overconfident means having more confidence than the situation justifies. Complacent means feeling so satisfied or secure that you stop noticing problems or making an effort.

Example: The candidate became overconfident and failed to prepare for the final interview.

Direct or useful antonyms

vigilant

adjective · Too satisfied to notice risks

Opposition: A vigilant person remains watchful for possible problems or danger, while a complacent person fails to give them enough attention.

Example: Health officials remained vigilant for signs of another outbreak.

diligent

adjective · Too satisfied to notice risks

Opposition: A diligent person continues to work carefully and consistently, while a complacent person may reduce their effort because they feel too secure.

Usage note: This is an opposite mainly when complacent refers to a lack of continued effort.

Example: The research team was diligent in checking every stage of the experiment.

Precision

Words learners may confuse

complaisant

adjective

Why it is confusing: The two words have similar spellings and pronunciations but different meanings.

Key difference: Complacent means too satisfied and therefore insufficiently careful. Complaisant means willing to please other people or agree with them.

complacent: The company became complacent after dominating the market for years.

complaisant: The complaisant assistant agreed to every request.

compliant

adjective

Why it is confusing: Both words begin with compl- and may occur in formal professional contexts.

Key difference: Complacent describes excessive satisfaction and lack of caution. Compliant describes someone or something that follows rules, requests, or standards.

complacent: Managers became complacent about the growing security threat.

compliant: The new equipment is fully compliant with safety regulations.

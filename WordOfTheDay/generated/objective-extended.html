Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

Based on facts rather than opinions

adjective /əbˈdʒɛktɪv/ neutral to formal; common in academic, scientific, legal, journalistic, and professional contexts
🔊 Hear adjective pronunciation

based on observable facts and evidence rather than personal opinions, feelings, or preferences

In simpler words: fair and based on facts

Typical pattern: objective + assessment, evidence, criterion, analysis, or judgment

Natural examples

  1. Researchers should remain objective when interpreting their results.
  2. The committee used objective criteria to evaluate every application.
  3. Journalists are expected to provide objective accounts of important events.
  4. The report offers an objective assessment of the policy's strengths and weaknesses.
  5. It is difficult to be completely objective when judging your own work.
  6. The selection process should be based on objective standards rather than personal connections.
  7. The judge must consider the evidence in an objective manner.
  8. Clear scoring rules can make essay evaluation more objective.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The word emphasizes reducing the influence of personal preference, emotion, or prejudice.

Usual implication: Complete objectivity may be difficult, but methods, evidence, and clear standards can make a judgment more objective.

Compared with “subjective”: Objective judgments rely mainly on observable evidence and agreed standards. Subjective judgments are influenced by personal opinions, feelings, or experiences.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it before a noun, as in objective evidence, or after a linking verb, as in The evaluation was objective.
What can follow it
It commonly appears before nouns such as assessment, analysis, evidence, criterion, standard, judgment, account, and evaluation.
Position in a sentence
It is often used after remain, seem, appear, or be when describing a person's judgment or approach.

Useful patterns

objective + assessment, analysis, or evaluation

Use this for an examination based mainly on evidence rather than personal preference.

Example: The review provides an objective evaluation of the new teaching method.

objective criteria or standards

Use this for clear rules that can be applied consistently to different people or cases.

Example: Applicants were selected according to objective criteria.

remain or try to be objective

Use this when someone attempts not to let personal feelings affect a judgment.

Example: The researcher tried to remain objective despite disagreeing with the theory.

make something more objective

Use this when evidence or clear rules reduce the influence of personal opinion.

Example: Anonymous grading may make the assessment process more objective.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The interviewer made an objective based on her personal preferences.

Correct: The interviewer made a subjective judgment based on her personal preferences.

A judgment based on personal preferences is subjective, not objective.

Wrong: The researcher answered the question objective.

Correct: The researcher answered the question objectively.

Use the adverb objectively to describe how someone performs an action.

Wrong: An objective report must contain no interpretation at all.

Correct: An objective report may include interpretation, but it should base that interpretation on evidence rather than personal preference.

Objective does not mean that analysis or interpretation is impossible.

Meaning 2

Independently observable or measurable

adjective /əbˈdʒɛktɪv/ formal; common in science, medicine, psychology, philosophy, economics, and research writing
🔊 Hear adjective pronunciation

existing outside a person's mind or able to be observed and measured independently

In simpler words: real or measurable outside personal experience

Typical pattern: objective + evidence, measurement, condition, sign, reality, or indicator

Natural examples

  1. The study combined patients' personal reports with objective measurements of sleep.
  2. Scientists searched for objective evidence that the treatment was effective.
  3. Income provides an objective measure of economic status, although it does not show every aspect of well-being.
  4. The physician found no objective signs of physical injury.
  5. Researchers compared subjective feelings of stress with objective changes in heart rate.
  6. Temperature is an objective property that can be measured with an instrument.
  7. The investigation focused on objective conditions rather than individual perceptions.
  8. The theory assumes that an objective reality exists independently of human observation.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: This sense contrasts external or independently measurable facts with internal feelings and personal perceptions.

Usual implication: Something objective can normally be checked by other people using observation, records, or measurement.

Compared with “subjective”: Objective information can be independently observed or measured. Subjective information describes a person's private experience, opinion, or perception.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it before a noun naming evidence, data, a measurement, a condition, or something believed to exist independently.
What can follow it
Common nouns include evidence, data, measure, measurement, indicator, sign, condition, fact, and reality.
Position in a sentence
In research writing, objective is often directly contrasted with subjective.

Useful patterns

objective evidence of + noun

Use this for observable information supporting a conclusion.

Example: The study found objective evidence of improvement.

objective measure of + noun

Use this for a measurement that does not depend mainly on personal judgment.

Example: Attendance provides an objective measure of student participation.

objective signs of + condition

Use this for symptoms or features that other people can observe or measure.

Example: The examination revealed no objective signs of infection.

subjective and objective data

Use this when comparing personal reports with independently measurable information.

Example: The researchers collected both subjective and objective data on fatigue.

objective reality

Use this for reality considered to exist independently of individual thought or perception.

Example: The philosophers disagreed about whether humans can fully understand objective reality.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The patient's personal opinion was an objective measurement.

Correct: The patient's personal opinion was a subjective report.

A personal opinion is subjective rather than independently measurable.

Wrong: The researchers measured the results objective.

Correct: The researchers measured the results objectively.

Use the adverb objectively to describe how something was measured.

Wrong: Objective evidence means evidence that proves a claim with complete certainty.

Correct: Objective evidence is independently observable evidence, but it may still be incomplete or open to interpretation.

Objective does not necessarily mean final or perfectly certain.

Meaning 3

Result to be achieved

countable noun /əbˈdʒɛktɪv/ neutral to formal; very common in academic, business, educational, governmental, and professional contexts
🔊 Hear countable noun pronunciation

a specific result that a person or organization intends to achieve

In simpler words: a specific goal

Typical pattern: the objective of + project or an objective is to + verb

Natural examples

  1. The primary objective of the program is to improve access to higher education.
  2. The research team achieved its main objective ahead of schedule.
  3. Before beginning the project, the students established several clear objectives.
  4. The policy failed to meet its stated objectives.
  5. Reducing energy consumption is one of the organization's long-term objectives.
  6. Each lesson begins with a clearly defined learning objective.
  7. The committee reviewed whether the original objectives were still realistic.
  8. The campaign has two major objectives: increasing awareness and changing public behavior.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: An objective is usually a clearly stated and practical result rather than a vague hope.

Usual implication: Objectives are often used to guide planning and to measure whether a project, policy, or activity has succeeded.

Compared with “aim”: Aim and objective are often close in meaning. Objective usually sounds more specific, formal, and measurable, while aim can be broader and less precisely defined.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use an objective for one intended result and objectives for several.
What can follow it
It is often followed by of and the project or organization, or by a form of be and to plus a verb.
Common preposition
Say the objective of the study, not the objective for the study, when identifying its main purpose. Use objective for when identifying the intended beneficiary, as in an objective for the next year.
Noun use
This is a countable noun, so the singular normally needs a, an, or the.

Useful patterns

the main or primary objective

Use this for the most important result that someone wants to achieve.

Example: The primary objective is to reduce preventable illness.

the objective of + project, study, or policy

Use this to identify the intended result of an organized activity.

Example: The objective of the study was to examine changes in migration patterns.

an objective is to + verb

Use this to state a goal as an action.

Example: One objective is to increase cooperation between departments.

achieve, meet, or accomplish an objective

Use this when someone successfully reaches an intended result.

Example: The project achieved all of its original objectives.

set or establish objectives

Use this when deciding what a project or organization should achieve.

Example: The team established clear objectives before beginning the experiment.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The objective of the program is improving access to education.

Correct: The objective of the program is to improve access to education.

After the objective is, the most common pattern is to followed by the base form of a verb.

Wrong: The team achieved to its main objective.

Correct: The team achieved its main objective.

Achieve takes the objective directly and is not followed by to.

Wrong: The project has an objective to reducing waste.

Correct: The project has an objective of reducing waste.

Use an objective of followed by an -ing form, or say the objective is to reduce waste.

/ˌɑːbdʒɛkˈtɪvəti/

Pattern: maintain, improve, ensure, or question + objectivity

  2. Anonymous review can help improve objectivity in the selection process.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

impartial

adjective · Based on facts rather than opinions

Shared meaning: Both words can describe fair judgment that is not controlled by personal preference.

Difference: Impartial emphasizes treating different people or sides fairly without favoring one. Objective more broadly emphasizes relying on facts rather than personal feelings.

Example: The dispute was examined by an impartial committee.

unbiased

adjective · Based on facts rather than opinions

Shared meaning: Both words describe judgment that avoids unfair personal influence.

Difference: Unbiased specifically means not unfairly favoring one side. Objective also emphasizes the use of observable evidence and clear standards.

Example: The researchers attempted to select an unbiased sample.

measurable

adjective · Independently observable or measurable

Shared meaning: Both can describe information that does not depend only on personal opinion.

Difference: Measurable means capable of being measured. Objective is broader and may describe any fact or condition that can be independently observed or verified.

Example: The policy produced measurable improvements in air quality.

goal

countable noun · Result to be achieved

Shared meaning: Both words refer to something that a person or organization wants to achieve.

Difference: Goal is the common everyday term. Objective is more formal and often refers to a specific, planned, or measurable result.

Example: The team's long-term goal is to develop a low-cost vaccine.

aim

countable noun · Result to be achieved

Shared meaning: Both words can name the intended result of an activity.

Difference: Aim can be broad or general. Objective often suggests a more clearly defined result that can be used to evaluate success.

Example: The main aim of the course is to develop critical thinking.

Direct or useful antonyms

subjective

adjective · Based on facts rather than opinions

Opposition: Subjective judgments depend on personal opinions, feelings, or preferences, while objective judgments rely mainly on observable facts and agreed standards.

Example: Judgments about beauty are often subjective.

subjective

adjective · Independently observable or measurable

Opposition: Subjective information describes personal experience or perception, while objective information can be independently observed or measured.

Example: Pain intensity is partly subjective because each patient experiences it differently.

Precision

Words learners may confuse

subjective

adjective

Why it is confusing: The words are commonly contrasted in academic and research contexts.

Key difference: Objective means based on independently observable facts or measurements. Subjective means influenced by personal opinion, feeling, or experience.

objective: The researchers used objective measurements of physical activity.

subjective: Participants also provided subjective reports of how tired they felt.

objection

countable noun

Why it is confusing: The words share the same beginning and are both common in formal writing.

Key difference: An objective is a goal. An objection is a reason for opposing or disagreeing with something.

objective: The project's main objective is to reduce pollution.

objection: Residents raised an objection to the proposed factory.

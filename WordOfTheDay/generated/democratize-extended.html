Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

Make more democratic

transitive verb /dɪˈmɑːkrəˌtaɪz/ formal; common in politics, history, sociology, and discussions of organizations
🔊 Hear transitive verb pronunciation

to make a country, organization, or system more democratic by giving people more power or a greater role in decisions

In simpler words: give more people a real voice in decisions

Typical pattern: democratize + country, institution, system, or decision-making

Natural examples

  1. The reforms were intended to democratize the country's political system.
  2. Student representatives argued that the university should democratize its decision-making process.
  3. The new constitution helped democratize several public institutions.
  4. Local councils were created to democratize the management of community resources.
  5. The movement sought to democratize the workplace by giving employees a stronger voice.
  6. Critics questioned whether the changes would truly democratize the organization.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The word suggests moving power or decision-making away from a small group and toward wider participation.

Usual implication: It often describes a process rather than a single immediate change.

Compared with “liberalize”: To liberalize is to reduce restrictions or government control. To democratize is specifically to increase democratic participation, representation, or accountability.

Grammar notes

How to use it
This verb normally needs the country, organization, system, or process that is being changed after it.
What can follow it
It is often followed by words such as government, society, institution, system, workplace, decision-making, or governance.
Position in a sentence
It is also common in the passive form, as in The institution was gradually democratized.

Useful patterns

democratize + institution or system

Use this when reforms give more people influence over how an organization or system operates.

Example: The proposal aims to democratize the national education system.

democratize decision-making

Use this when a wider group is given a meaningful role in making choices.

Example: The committee introduced open meetings to democratize decision-making.

be democratized

Use the passive form when the change itself is more important than the person or group causing it.

Example: The organization was gradually democratized through a series of internal reforms.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The reform democratized people to make decisions.

Correct: The reform democratized the decision-making process.

Democratize is normally followed by the system or process that becomes more democratic, not by people plus to.

Wrong: The government wants to democratize in the public institutions.

Correct: The government wants to democratize the public institutions.

In this meaning, the verb normally takes its object directly, without in.

Meaning 2

Widen access

transitive verb /dɪˈmɑːkrəˌtaɪz/ formal and increasingly common in education, technology, business, and public policy
🔊 Hear transitive verb pronunciation

to make information, education, technology, or another useful resource available to many more people instead of only a small or powerful group

In simpler words: make something available to far more people

Typical pattern: democratize access to + resource or democratize + knowledge, education, technology, or opportunity

Natural examples

  1. Online libraries can democratize access to academic research.
  2. Low-cost smartphones have helped democratize access to digital services.
  3. The museum hopes to democratize art by offering free virtual exhibitions.
  4. Open-source software can democratize advanced technology for small organizations.
  5. Public data projects aim to democratize information that was once difficult to obtain.
  6. Some educators believe artificial intelligence could democratize personalized learning.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: This use is not necessarily about elections or government. It describes removing barriers that kept a valuable resource within a limited group.

Usual implication: The barriers may involve cost, location, technical knowledge, social position, or institutional control.

Compared with “popularize”: To popularize something is to make it widely known or liked. To democratize it is to make access or participation more widely available.

Grammar notes

How to use it
This verb normally needs the resource, opportunity, or type of access that is being made more widely available after it.
What can follow it
Common objects include access, education, knowledge, information, technology, finance, culture, and opportunity.
Common preposition
A very common pattern is democratize access to something, as in democratize access to higher education.
Position in a sentence
The phrase help democratize is common when one development contributes to wider access but is not the only cause.

Useful patterns

democratize access to + noun

Use this when more people are given a realistic chance to obtain or use something.

Example: Digital courses may democratize access to professional training.

democratize + knowledge, technology, or education

Use this when the resource itself becomes less limited to experts, wealthy users, or major institutions.

Example: The platform was designed to democratize financial knowledge.

help democratize + noun

Use this when something contributes to broader access.

Example: Affordable equipment could help democratize scientific research.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The website democratizes the access to legal information.

Correct: The website democratizes access to legal information.

Access is normally used without the in this pattern.

Wrong: The program democratized education to rural students.

Correct: The program democratized access to education for rural students.

Use access to for the resource and for to identify the people who benefit.

Morphology

Word family

Only common and educationally useful family members are included.

uncountable noun

democratization

/dɪˌmɑːkrətəˈzeɪʃən/

the process of making a country, organization, system, or resource more democratic or more widely accessible

Relationship: This noun names the process or result of democratizing something.

Pattern: the democratization of + country, institution, system, knowledge, or access

Usage note: It is usually used without a or an.

  1. The report examines the democratization of local government.
  2. Digital tools have contributed to the democratization of knowledge.

adjective

democratic

/ˌdɛməˈkrætɪk/

based on democracy or giving people a fair and meaningful role in decisions

Relationship: This adjective describes a system, process, or organization that has democratic qualities.

Pattern: democratic + government, election, institution, process, or society

  1. The country held its first democratic election in decades.
  2. Employees called for a more democratic workplace.

countable or uncountable noun

democracy

/dɪˈmɑːkrəsi/

a system of government in which people choose their leaders and can take part in political decisions

Relationship: Democratize originally refers to making something more like a democracy.

Pattern: democracy or a democracy

Usage note: Use democracy without a number for the general idea and a democracy for a country or political system.

  1. Free elections are an important part of democracy.
  2. The transition to a stable democracy took many years.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

liberalize

transitive verb · Make more democratic

Shared meaning: Both words can describe reforms that make a system more open.

Difference: Liberalize means to reduce restrictions or controls. Such reforms may support democratization, but they do not necessarily give people more political power.

Example: The government liberalized restrictions on the press.

broaden access to

verb phrase · Widen access

Shared meaning: Both expressions mean making a resource available to more people.

Difference: Broaden access to is a more direct and neutral phrase. Democratize often adds the idea that access was previously controlled by a privileged or powerful group.

Example: The program broadened access to university courses.

popularize

transitive verb · Widen access

Shared meaning: Both can describe something reaching a much wider audience.

Difference: Popularize means to make something widely known, understood, or liked. Democratize focuses on removing barriers to access or participation.

Example: The television series popularized astronomy among young viewers.

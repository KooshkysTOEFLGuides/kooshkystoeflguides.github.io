Meanings and usage

Meaning 1

Happening at the same time

adjective /kənˈkɝːənt/ formal; common in academic, medical, technological, legal, and professional contexts
🔊 Hear adjective pronunciation

happening, existing, or being done at the same time as something else

In simpler words: happening at the same time

Typical pattern: concurrent with + event or concurrent + events, processes, or activities

Natural examples

  1. The university scheduled three concurrent workshops, so students had to choose one.
  2. The patient was treated for two concurrent infections.
  3. The software can perform several concurrent tasks without slowing down.
  4. The researchers monitored concurrent changes in temperature and humidity.
  5. Two concurrent construction projects caused severe traffic delays.
  6. She completed a degree while receiving concurrent professional training.
  7. The conference offered concurrent sessions on language learning and educational technology.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The word emphasizes that two or more things occur during the same period rather than one after another.

Usual implication: The events may begin and end at different moments, but at least part of their duration overlaps.

Compared with “consecutive”: Concurrent events happen at the same time, while consecutive events follow one another without occurring together.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it before a noun, as in concurrent events, or after a linking verb, as in The projects were concurrent.
What can follow it
It commonly appears before words such as event, session, process, task, illness, treatment, sentence, and program.
Common preposition
Use concurrent with when comparing one event or condition with another, as in The training was concurrent with her university studies.
Position in a sentence
Concurrent usually appears directly before the noun it describes.

Useful patterns

concurrent + events, sessions, or processes

Use this for activities or developments occurring during the same period.

Example: Delegates could choose between four concurrent sessions.

be concurrent with + noun

Use this to state that one event happens during the same period as another.

Example: The population decline was concurrent with a prolonged drought.

run or occur concurrently

Use the adverb concurrently when describing how two or more activities happen.

Example: The two experiments were conducted concurrently.

concurrent sentences

In law, this describes prison sentences that are served at the same time rather than one after another.

Example: The judge ordered the two prison sentences to run concurrently.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The two courses are concurrent to each other.

Correct: The two courses are concurrent with each other.

Use concurrent with, not concurrent to.

Wrong: The first lecture ended, and then a concurrent lecture began.

Correct: The first lecture ended, and then a consecutive lecture began.

Concurrent means happening at the same time. Use consecutive when one event follows another.

Wrong: The meetings happened concurrent.

Correct: The meetings happened concurrently.

Use the adverb concurrently to describe how something happened.

Meaning 2

Shared by multiple authorities

adjective /kənˈkɝːənt/ formal and specialized; especially common in law, government, and administration
🔊 Hear adjective pronunciation

held or exercised at the same time by two or more authorities, organizations, or people

In simpler words: belonging to more than one authority at once

Typical pattern: concurrent jurisdiction, powers, authority, control, or responsibility

Natural examples

  1. Federal and state courts may have concurrent jurisdiction over certain cases.
  2. The constitution gives the national and regional governments some concurrent powers.
  3. Both departments have concurrent responsibility for protecting the site.
  4. The agencies exercise concurrent authority over environmental regulation.
  5. The agreement established concurrent control of the shared research facility.
  6. Several institutions have concurrent roles in managing the public health system.
  7. The two committees were given concurrent responsibility for reviewing the proposal.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The word indicates that the same type of power, control, or responsibility exists in more than one place at the same time.

Usual implication: The authorities may act separately within the same area rather than always acting as a single combined group.

Compared with “exclusive”: Concurrent authority is held by more than one body, while exclusive authority belongs to only one body.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it before a noun naming the shared power, legal right, or responsibility.
What can follow it
Common nouns after it include jurisdiction, authority, power, control, responsibility, and role.
Common preposition
Jurisdiction and authority are often followed by over, as in concurrent jurisdiction over the dispute.
Position in a sentence
In this meaning, concurrent normally appears directly before the noun it describes.

Useful patterns

concurrent jurisdiction over + matter

Use this when two or more courts or governments have legal power over the same type of case.

Example: The two courts have concurrent jurisdiction over the dispute.

concurrent powers

Use this for powers that more than one level of government may exercise.

Example: Collecting taxes is a concurrent power in some federal systems.

concurrent responsibility for + noun or -ing form

Use this when two or more groups share responsibility for something.

Example: The ministries have concurrent responsibility for managing the program.

concurrent authority over + area

Use this when several bodies possess authority in the same area.

Example: The agencies exercise concurrent authority over workplace safety.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The courts have a concurrent jurisdiction over the case.

Correct: The courts have concurrent jurisdiction over the case.

Jurisdiction is normally used without a in this expression.

Wrong: Both agencies have concurrent authorities over the industry.

Correct: Both agencies have concurrent authority over the industry.

Authority is normally uncountable when it means official power.

Wrong: Only one department has concurrent control of the system.

Correct: Two departments have concurrent control of the system.

Concurrent control requires control to be held by more than one party.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

simultaneous

adjective · Happening at the same time

Shared meaning: Both words describe events occurring at the same time.

Difference: Simultaneous often emphasizes that events occur at exactly the same moment. Concurrent can describe events that overlap during the same general period, even if their starting and ending times differ.

Example: The two explosions were almost simultaneous.

overlapping

adjective · Happening at the same time

Shared meaning: Both can describe events whose time periods occur together.

Difference: Overlapping emphasizes that only part of the time period is shared. Concurrent more generally means occurring during the same period.

Example: The students had two overlapping classes on Monday.

parallel

adjective · Happening at the same time

Shared meaning: Both can describe activities proceeding at the same time.

Difference: Parallel processes develop or operate beside each other and may be similar or connected. Concurrent only requires them to occur during the same period.

Example: The researchers conducted parallel investigations in three countries.

shared

adjective · Shared by multiple authorities

Shared meaning: Both can describe power or responsibility belonging to more than one party.

Difference: Shared is a broad everyday term. Concurrent is more formal and often means that each authority independently possesses the same power or responsibility.

Example: The two departments have shared responsibility for the project.

Direct or useful antonyms

consecutive

adjective · Happening at the same time

Opposition: Consecutive events occur one after another, while concurrent events occur during the same period.

Example: The interviews were held on three consecutive days.

sequential

adjective · Happening at the same time

Opposition: Sequential processes follow a particular order, while concurrent processes operate during the same period.

Example: The stages must be completed in sequential order.

exclusive

adjective · Shared by multiple authorities

Opposition: Exclusive authority belongs to only one party, while concurrent authority is held by more than one.

Example: The national court has exclusive jurisdiction over these cases.

Precision

Words learners may confuse

consecutive

adjective

Why it is confusing: Both words are formal adjectives often used to describe the timing or order of events.

Key difference: Concurrent means happening at the same time, while consecutive means happening one after another.

concurrent: The festival held several concurrent performances.

consecutive: The theater presented performances on five consecutive evenings.

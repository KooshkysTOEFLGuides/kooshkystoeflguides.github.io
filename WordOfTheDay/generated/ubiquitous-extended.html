Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

Present almost everywhere

adjective /juːˈbɪkwətəs/ neutral to formal; common in academic, scientific, technological, and social discussions
🔊 Hear adjective pronunciation

present, found, or appearing almost everywhere, especially within a particular place, society, or situation

In simpler words: almost everywhere

Typical pattern: ubiquitous in, across, or throughout + place or area

Natural examples

  1. Smartphones have become ubiquitous in modern society.
  2. Plastic is now ubiquitous in the world's oceans.
  3. Coffee shops are ubiquitous throughout the city.
  4. The researchers found that the bacteria were ubiquitous in the local environment.
  5. Advertising has become ubiquitous on social media platforms.
  6. Security cameras are increasingly ubiquitous in public spaces.
  7. The plant is ubiquitous across the region because it can survive in many different climates.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The word does not usually mean literally present in every possible place. It emphasizes that something is extremely widespread and difficult to avoid or overlook.

Usual implication: It often suggests that something has become a normal or noticeable part of an environment or society.

Compared with “widespread”: Widespread means existing in many places or affecting many people. Ubiquitous is stronger and suggests that something seems to be almost everywhere.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it before a noun or after a linking verb such as be, become, or seem.
What can follow it
When it comes after a linking verb, it is often followed by in, across, or throughout and then a place, field, or situation.
Common preposition
Use ubiquitous in for an environment or area, as in ubiquitous in modern life. Across and throughout are also common when emphasizing geographical spread.
Position in a sentence
You can say ubiquitous technology before a noun, or Technology has become ubiquitous after a linking verb.

Useful patterns

ubiquitous + noun

Use this before a noun to describe something that is present almost everywhere.

Example: The study examines the effects of ubiquitous digital technology.

be or become ubiquitous

Use this when something is or becomes extremely common in many places.

Example: Wireless internet has become ubiquitous on university campuses.

ubiquitous in + place or situation

Use this to identify the environment in which something is found almost everywhere.

Example: These symbols are ubiquitous in religious art.

ubiquitous across or throughout + area

Use this to emphasize that something is widely present over a large area.

Example: The species is ubiquitous throughout the coastal region.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: Mobile phones are ubiquitous everywhere.

Correct: Mobile phones are ubiquitous.

Ubiquitous already means almost everywhere, so everywhere is usually unnecessary.

Wrong: This technology is an ubiquitous feature of modern life.

Correct: This technology is a ubiquitous feature of modern life.

Use a because ubiquitous begins with the consonant sound /j/, like the first sound in university.

Wrong: Online advertising has ubiquitous in modern life.

Correct: Online advertising has become ubiquitous in modern life.

Ubiquitous is an adjective, so use a linking verb such as become before it.

uncountable noun

ubiquity

/juːˈbɪkwəti/

the state of being present or found almost everywhere

Relationship: This noun names the state or quality described by ubiquitous.

Pattern: the ubiquity of + noun

Usage note: It is formal and is usually used in the expression the ubiquity of something.

  1. The ubiquity of smartphones has changed how people communicate.
  2. Researchers are concerned about the ubiquity of plastic waste in marine environments.

adverb

ubiquitously

/juːˈbɪkwətəsli/

in a way that is present or occurs almost everywhere

Relationship: This adverb describes how widely something exists or occurs.

Usage note: It is much less common than ubiquitous and is mainly used in formal writing.

  1. The software is now used ubiquitously across the industry.
  2. The symbol appears ubiquitously in the artist's later work.

Synonyms and antonyms

Close synonyms

widespread

adjective · Present almost everywhere

Shared meaning: Both words describe something that exists across a large area or among many people.

Difference: Widespread means existing in many places or affecting many people. Ubiquitous is stronger and suggests that something seems to be almost everywhere.

Example: The new policy received widespread public support.

pervasive

adjective · Present almost everywhere

Shared meaning: Both words can describe something present throughout an environment.

Difference: Pervasive often suggests that something spreads through and influences every part of a place, system, or situation. It is frequently used for problems, attitudes, smells, or influences. Ubiquitous focuses more simply on being found almost everywhere.

Example: The report identified a pervasive culture of secrecy within the organization.

omnipresent

adjective · Present almost everywhere

Shared meaning: Both words express the idea of being present everywhere or almost everywhere.

Difference: Omnipresent literally suggests being present everywhere and is often used for a god, authority, influence, or something that feels impossible to escape. Ubiquitous is more common in academic descriptions of widely present objects or phenomena.

Example: The ruler's image was omnipresent throughout the capital.

common

adjective · Present almost everywhere

Shared meaning: Both words can describe something frequently encountered.

Difference: Common means frequently found or occurring. Ubiquitous is much stronger and emphasizes extremely wide presence.

Example: This bird is common in urban parks.

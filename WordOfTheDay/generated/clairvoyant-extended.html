Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

Able to perceive hidden or future events

adjective /klɛrˈvɔɪənt/ neutral to formal; often used in discussions of supernatural beliefs, fiction, and psychological research
🔊 Hear adjective pronunciation

believed to be able to know about future, distant, or hidden events without receiving information through the ordinary senses

In simpler words: supposedly able to know hidden things or see the future

Typical pattern: be clairvoyant or have clairvoyant abilities

Natural examples

  1. She claimed to be clairvoyant and able to predict major events.
  2. The story features a clairvoyant woman who sees visions of the future.
  3. I am not clairvoyant, so you need to tell me what you are thinking.
  4. The researchers tested participants who said they had clairvoyant abilities.
  5. According to the legend, the priest was clairvoyant and could see events occurring in distant lands.
  6. He jokingly asked whether she was clairvoyant after her prediction came true.
  7. The novel's main character becomes clairvoyant after a mysterious accident.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The word refers to knowledge supposedly gained without normal observation, communication, or reasoning.

Usual implication: Writers often use claimed, believed, or supposedly because clairvoyant ability has not been scientifically established.

Compared with “observant”: An observant person notices information through the ordinary senses. A clairvoyant person is claimed to receive information without those normal sources.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it after a linking verb such as be or seem, or before a noun such as ability, vision, or character.
What can follow it
It may be followed by enough context to explain the supposed ability, as in clairvoyant about future events, although this pattern is less common than have clairvoyant abilities.
Position in a sentence
The expression I am not clairvoyant is often used humorously to mean that someone cannot know unspoken information or predict what will happen.

Useful patterns

be clairvoyant

Use this to describe someone who is believed or claimed to have the ability.

Example: The fortune-teller claimed to be clairvoyant.

clairvoyant ability

Use this to name the supposed power to perceive hidden or future events.

Example: The study found no reliable evidence of clairvoyant ability.

I am not clairvoyant

Use this, often humorously, when someone expects you to know something they have not told you.

Example: I am not clairvoyant; please explain what you need.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: She is a clairvoyant person who can clairvoyance the future.

Correct: She is a clairvoyant person who claims to see the future.

Clairvoyance is a noun, not a verb.

Wrong: I am not a clairvoyant, so tell me what you want.

Correct: I am not clairvoyant, so tell me what you want.

Use the adjective without a when describing an ability. I am not a clairvoyant is possible only when clairvoyant means a person who practices professionally.

Meaning 2

Showing accurate foresight

adjective /klɛrˈvɔɪənt/ formal and often figurative; common in commentary, reviews, and historical analysis
🔊 Hear adjective pronunciation

showing an unusually accurate understanding of what will happen or of something that is not yet obvious

In simpler words: extremely accurate about what would happen

Typical pattern: seem, appear, or prove clairvoyant

Natural examples

  1. His warning about the economic crisis now seems almost clairvoyant.
  2. The author's clairvoyant analysis anticipated many later technological changes.
  3. Her prediction that remote work would become widespread appeared remarkably clairvoyant.
  4. In retrospect, the committee's concerns about water shortages seem clairvoyant.
  5. The novel offered a nearly clairvoyant picture of life in a highly connected society.
  6. His clairvoyant assessment of the market allowed the company to prepare early.
  7. What once sounded unrealistic now appears to have been a clairvoyant observation.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: This use does not necessarily suggest a real supernatural power. It emphasizes that an earlier prediction or judgment was surprisingly accurate.

Usual implication: The accuracy often becomes clear only later, after the predicted development has occurred.

Compared with “prescient”: Prescient also means accurately anticipating future events and is usually the more precise formal choice. Clairvoyant is more vivid and may suggest that the accuracy seemed almost supernatural.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it after seem, appear, or prove, or before nouns such as prediction, warning, analysis, or observation.
What can follow it
The sentence usually explains the later event that made the earlier prediction appear accurate.
Position in a sentence
Modifiers such as almost, nearly, remarkably, and surprisingly are common because this use is usually figurative.

Useful patterns

seem or appear clairvoyant

Use this when an earlier judgment looks unusually accurate after later events.

Example: Her comments from a decade ago now seem clairvoyant.

an almost clairvoyant prediction

Use this for a prediction that was so accurate that it seems supernatural.

Example: The report contained an almost clairvoyant prediction of the energy crisis.

a clairvoyant analysis or warning

Use this for an analysis or warning that correctly anticipated later developments.

Example: His clairvoyant warning was ignored by policymakers at the time.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The prediction was clairvoyance.

Correct: The prediction was clairvoyant.

Use the adjective clairvoyant after was. Clairvoyance is a noun.

Wrong: Her analysis clairvoyantly the future.

Correct: Her analysis predicted the future with remarkable accuracy.

Clairvoyantly is an adverb and cannot function as the main verb.

Meaning 3

Person believed to have supernatural perception

countable noun /klɛrˈvɔɪənt/ neutral; common in fiction, popular culture, and discussions of supernatural claims
🔊 Hear countable noun pronunciation

a person who claims or is believed to know about future, distant, or hidden events through a supernatural ability

In simpler words: a person said to see hidden things or the future

Typical pattern: a clairvoyant who claims to + verb

Natural examples

  1. The clairvoyant claimed that she could describe events occurring far away.
  2. He visited a clairvoyant who offered to predict his future.
  3. The experiment tested whether self-described clairvoyants could identify hidden objects.
  4. In the novel, a young clairvoyant warns the village about an approaching disaster.
  5. The documentary interviewed several people who worked professionally as clairvoyants.
  6. The police rejected the clairvoyant's offer to help locate the missing property.
  7. Skeptics argued that the clairvoyant had obtained the information through ordinary questioning.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The noun identifies a person associated specifically with perceiving distant, hidden, or future events.

Usual implication: The word itself does not prove that the person's claims are true, so careful writing often includes claimed, alleged, or self-described.

Compared with “fortune-teller”: A fortune-teller predicts a person's future, often as a profession. A clairvoyant is believed to perceive hidden, distant, or future information and may claim a broader type of supernatural perception.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use a clairvoyant for one person and clairvoyants for more than one.
What can follow it
It is often followed by a clause beginning with who to explain what the person claims to perceive.
Noun use
This is a countable noun, so the singular normally needs a, an, or the.

Useful patterns

a clairvoyant who claims to + verb

Use this to report the person's claim without presenting it as an established fact.

Example: The program featured a clairvoyant who claimed to communicate with distant people.

a self-described clairvoyant

Use this for someone who identifies themselves as having such abilities.

Example: A self-described clairvoyant agreed to participate in the controlled experiment.

consult or visit a clairvoyant

Use this when someone seeks advice or predictions from such a person.

Example: The character visits a clairvoyant before beginning her journey.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: She works as clairvoyant.

Correct: She works as a clairvoyant.

The singular countable noun needs a.

Wrong: The clairvoyance predicted a major change.

Correct: The clairvoyant predicted a major change.

Clairvoyant refers to the person. Clairvoyance refers to the supposed ability.

Pattern: evidence of clairvoyance or claim to possess clairvoyance

Usage note: It is normally used without a or an.

  2. Clairvoyance plays an important role in the novel's plot.

adverb

clairvoyantly

/klɛrˈvɔɪəntli/

in a way that seems to show knowledge of future or hidden events

Relationship: This adverb describes an action that appears unusually accurate about future or hidden events.

Usage note: It is uncommon. A phrase such as with remarkable foresight is often more natural.

  1. The author almost clairvoyantly anticipated the rise of digital surveillance.
  2. She seemed to predict the change clairvoyantly.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

psychic

adjective · Able to perceive hidden or future events

Shared meaning: Both words can describe a person believed to receive information through supernatural means.

Difference: Psychic is broader and can refer to several supposed supernatural mental abilities. Clairvoyant refers more specifically to perceiving distant, hidden, or future events.

Example: She claimed to possess psychic abilities.

prescient

adjective · Showing accurate foresight

Shared meaning: Both words can describe a prediction or judgment that accurately anticipates later events.

Difference: Prescient means showing accurate knowledge of what will happen and does not usually suggest supernatural powers. Clairvoyant is more vivid and may imply that the accuracy seemed almost supernatural.

Example: The report's prescient warning was largely ignored at the time.

prophetic

adjective · Showing accurate foresight

Shared meaning: Both can describe words or predictions that later appear unusually accurate.

Difference: Prophetic often refers to a statement that correctly predicts the future and may have a religious or solemn association. Clairvoyant emphasizes apparently seeing what was hidden or still to come.

Example: Her comments about the environmental crisis proved prophetic.

fortune-teller

countable noun · Person believed to have supernatural perception

Shared meaning: Both words can refer to someone who claims to predict future events.

Difference: A fortune-teller specifically predicts people's futures, often as a service. A clairvoyant may claim to perceive a wider range of hidden, distant, or future events.

Example: The fortune-teller offered to read his cards.

medium

countable noun · Person believed to have supernatural perception

Shared meaning: Both words refer to people associated with claimed supernatural abilities.

Difference: A medium claims to communicate with the spirits of dead people. A clairvoyant claims to perceive hidden, distant, or future information.

Example: The medium claimed to deliver messages from the dead.

Precision

Words learners may confuse

clairvoyance

adjective or countable noun versus uncountable noun

Why it is confusing: The words have closely related meanings and very similar forms.

Key difference: Clairvoyant is an adjective or a noun referring to a person, while clairvoyance is the uncountable noun for the supposed ability.

clairvoyant: The novel describes a clairvoyant child.

clairvoyance: The novel explores the idea of clairvoyance.

Word history

Etymology

Only confidently documented details are shown.

Origin

The word comes from French clairvoyant, formed from words meaning clear and seeing.

Early meaning

Its basic historical sense was clear-sighted or able to see clearly.

Development

It later became associated specifically with the supposed supernatural perception of distant, hidden, or future events.

Meanings & Usage

Meaning 1 adjective /əˈmiːnəbəl/

Willing to accept an idea, suggestion, plan, rule, or request; open to agreement or cooperation.

Natural examples

  1. The professor was amenable to extending the deadline after several students explained the situation.
  2. The committee seemed amenable to a compromise that would satisfy both groups.
  3. Our landlord is usually amenable to reasonable requests if we explain them clearly.
  4. The manager was not amenable to changing the meeting time because the schedule was already full.
  5. Students are more amenable to feedback when they understand how it will improve their work.

Nuance & implication

Amenable is a fairly formal and academic word. It suggests polite willingness, flexibility, or readiness to listen. It is stronger and more precise than simply saying nice or okay with. It often appears when people, institutions, or decision-makers are open to discussion, advice, compromise, or change.

Grammar notes

Amenable is an adjective, so it usually comes after be, seem, remain, or become. The most common pattern is amenable to + noun / gerund: amenable to compromise, amenable to suggestion, amenable to changing the plan. Do not use it before a direct object; it is not a verb.

Common wrong usage

  • ❌ The professor amenabled the deadline. → ✅ The professor was amenable to extending the deadline.
  • ❌ She is amenable with my idea. → ✅ She is amenable to my idea.

TOEFL tip

In listening passages, amenable to can signal that a person is willing to negotiate or accept a solution. Pay attention to the object after to; it tells you what the person is open to.

Meaning 2 adjective /əˈmiːnəbəl/

Able to be treated, changed, influenced, studied, analyzed, or dealt with in a particular way.

Natural examples

  1. The disease is often amenable to treatment if doctors discover it early.
  2. The data set is amenable to statistical analysis because it contains clear numerical patterns.
  3. Some environmental problems are more amenable to local solutions than national ones.
  4. The clay soil became more amenable to farming after organic material was added.
  5. This question is amenable to experimental study because researchers can test it in a controlled setting.

Nuance & implication

This meaning is common in academic and scientific writing. It does not mean that a person is willing. Instead, it means that a problem, material, disease, data set, or question can be successfully handled by a method. The word often suggests that the thing is suitable for a particular process.

Grammar notes

The pattern is still amenable to + noun, but the subject is usually a thing, not a person: amenable to treatment, amenable to analysis, amenable to change, amenable to reform. In this meaning, to is a preposition, so use a noun or gerund after it, not a base verb.

Common wrong usage

  • ❌ The disease is amenable to treat. → ✅ The disease is amenable to treatment.
  • ❌ The data is amenable for analysis. → ✅ The data is amenable to analysis.

TOEFL tip

In reading passages, amenable to analysis/treatment/change often means that a method can work. If you see this phrase, identify the method after to; it usually tells you how the problem or material can be handled.

Word Family

amenable

Part of speech: adjective

Definition: willing to accept something, or able to be dealt with in a particular way

  1. The director was amenable to small changes in the proposal.
  2. The problem is amenable to a simple mathematical solution.

amenability

Part of speech: noun

Definition: the quality of being willing to accept something, or the quality of being suitable for treatment, analysis, or change

  1. The manager's amenability to feedback improved communication in the office.
  2. The study examined the soil's amenability to different farming methods.

Common Collocations

amenable to suggestion

Meaning: willing to listen to other people's ideas

Example: The instructor was amenable to suggestion and changed the project guidelines after hearing student concerns.

amenable to compromise

Meaning: willing to find a middle solution

Example: Both sides were amenable to compromise after several hours of discussion.

amenable to change

Meaning: willing to change, or able to be changed

Example: Younger institutions are often more amenable to change than older, more rigid ones.

amenable to persuasion

Meaning: willing to be convinced by reasons or evidence

Example: The board was not enthusiastic at first, but it proved amenable to persuasion.

amenable to feedback

Meaning: willing to accept comments and improve

Example: Students who are amenable to feedback usually make faster progress in writing.

amenable to treatment

Meaning: able to be treated successfully

Example: The condition is more amenable to treatment when it is diagnosed early.

amenable to analysis

Meaning: suitable for careful study or examination

Example: The survey results were amenable to analysis because the questions were clearly designed.

amenable to reform

Meaning: able or willing to be improved by changes

Example: The school system was more amenable to reform after parents and teachers agreed on the main problems.

readily amenable to

Meaning: easily suitable for a method, change, or process

Example: The text is readily amenable to digital analysis because it is already in a clean electronic format.

prove amenable to

Meaning: turn out to be willing or suitable for something

Example: The material proved amenable to recycling after researchers developed a new process.

Direct Synonyms & Antonyms

Direct synonyms

open to receptive to willing to consider responsive to suitable for

Open to is the simplest synonym for people who may accept an idea. Receptive to is close and slightly formal. Willing to consider is clear and useful in speaking. For things, responsive to and suitable for are often closer, especially in phrases like amenable to treatment or amenable to analysis.

Direct antonyms

resistant to opposed to unwilling unresponsive to unsuitable for

Resistant to is the most useful opposite because it works for people, systems, diseases, and materials. Opposed to and unwilling describe people who do not want to accept something. Unresponsive to and unsuitable for are better for things, methods, treatments, or analysis.

Etymology

Amenable came into English through French. It is connected to an older French verb meaning “to lead or bring,” and it first had a legal sense of being answerable or able to be brought before authority. The modern meanings developed from that idea of being responsive or subject to something: a person can be amenable to advice, and a problem can be amenable to treatment or analysis.

Practice Exercises

1. Word family choice

Choose the best member of the word family for each blank.

  1. The director was __________ to a small change in the schedule.
  2. The study measured the material's __________ to recycling.
  3. The problem proved __________ to statistical analysis.

2. Correct or incorrect?

Decide whether each sentence uses the word correctly. If it is wrong, correct it.

  1. The professor was amenable to discussing the deadline.
  2. The data was amenable for analysis.
  3. The committee amenabled the proposal after the meeting.

3. Collocation practice

Complete each sentence with a natural collocation.

  1. The illness is more __________ when doctors find it early.
  2. The two groups were finally __________ after a long debate.
  3. The survey results were __________ because the responses were easy to compare.
Answer Key
  1. amenable — The adjective is needed after was.
  2. amenability — The noun is needed after the possessive material's.
  3. amenable — The phrase prove amenable to means “turn out to be suitable for.”
  4. Correct. Amenable to discussing is natural because to is followed by a gerund.
  5. Incorrect. Better: The data was amenable to analysis. The correct preposition is to.
  6. Incorrect. Better: The committee was amenable to the proposal after the meeting. Amenable is an adjective, not a verb.
  7. amenable to treatment — This means able to be treated successfully.
  8. amenable to compromise — This means willing to find a middle solution.
  9. amenable to analysis — This means suitable for careful study or examination.

Quick orientation

Usage overview

Core idea

The central idea is helping something or someone grow, develop, or receive care.

Register

The development sense is common in neutral and formal English. The child-care senses are standard terms used in everyday, social-service, and legal contexts.

Where it appears

Learners often meet foster in discussions of education, cooperation, creativity, economic growth, community relations, and child welfare.

TOEFL value

Foster is useful for understanding cause-and-effect relationships and for writing about conditions that encourage positive development.

Sound

Pronunciation

Common American variant with the cot-caught merger

/ˈfɑstər/

Many American speakers pronounce the first vowel like the vowel in father.

Learner tip

Stress and sounds

Stress the first syllable. In American English, the final r is pronounced.

Dictionary guide

Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

Encourage development

transitive verb /ˈfɔstər/ Neutral to formal; especially common in academic, professional, and policy-related contexts.

to encourage the growth or development of something, especially a quality, activity, or relationship

In simpler words: help something grow or develop

Typical pattern: foster plus noun phrase

Common contexts

educationresearchinnovationcooperationeconomic developmentsocial relationshipspersonal qualities

Natural examples

  1. Open discussion can foster trust among members of a research team.
  2. The school created a mentoring program to foster students' interest in science.
  3. Public libraries foster learning by giving communities access to books and digital resources.
  4. Regular contact between the two groups helped foster mutual understanding.
  5. The new policy is intended to foster innovation in small businesses.
  6. Cooperative tasks can foster a stronger sense of responsibility in the classroom.
  7. The museum uses interactive exhibits to foster curiosity about the natural world.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: Foster suggests creating conditions that help something develop over time rather than causing an immediate result.

Usual implication: The object is often something positive, such as trust, creativity, cooperation, or growth.

Compared with “cause”: Cause is a broad word for producing a result. Foster specifically suggests supporting a gradual process of growth or development.

Grammar notes

Transitivity
This sense is transitive and normally takes a direct object.
Complements and objects
The object is usually a noun phrase naming a quality, process, activity, relationship, or form of development.
Prepositions
Foster itself does not require a fixed preposition. Prepositional phrases may identify the people or setting involved, as in foster trust among employees or foster interest in science.
Form and position
It is commonly used in active sentences, but passive forms are also natural when the supporting conditions are more important than the agent.

Useful patterns

foster plus abstract noun

Use this for qualities, relationships, activities, or processes that are encouraged to develop.

Example: The course is designed to foster independent thinking.

foster something among people

Use among to identify the members of a group in whom a quality or relationship develops.

Example: Shared projects can foster cooperation among students.

foster interest in something

Use this pattern when an activity encourages people to become more interested in a subject.

Example: The documentary fostered public interest in marine conservation.

be fostered by something

The passive pattern emphasizes the condition or action that supports development.

Example: Creativity is often fostered by an environment in which people can experiment safely.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The program fostered students to become more independent.

Correct: The program fostered greater independence among students.

In this sense, foster normally takes a noun or noun phrase, not an object followed by a to-infinitive.

Wrong: The policy fostered to economic growth.

Correct: The policy fostered economic growth.

Foster takes a direct object and does not require to before that object.

TOEFL use

Reading and listening

In academic passages and lectures, foster often signals that a condition supports a gradual positive development.

Writing and speaking

Use foster with precise nouns such as cooperation, curiosity, innovation, trust, or economic growth instead of the less specific phrase help something grow.

Paraphrase tip

Depending on context, foster can be paraphrased as encourage, promote, support the development of, or create conditions for.

Meaning 2

Provide temporary family care

transitive verb /ˈfɔstər/ Standard and neutral; common in discussions of families, social services, and child welfare.

to take a child into your home and care for them for a period of time without becoming their legal adoptive parent

In simpler words: care for a child temporarily in your home

Typical pattern: foster plus child or children

Common contexts

child welfaresocial servicestemporary family carefamily supportlegal and administrative processes

Natural examples

  1. The couple decided to foster two children while the children's mother recovered from a serious illness.
  2. Families who foster teenagers receive training and support from local agencies.
  3. She began fostering children after her own children had left home.
  4. The organization urgently needs more families willing to foster infants.
  5. They fostered the boy for nearly a year before he returned to his relatives.
  6. Some people foster several children at different times rather than adopting permanently.
  7. The agency carefully evaluates adults who apply to foster a child.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The care is temporary or officially arranged and is different from legal adoption.

Usual implication: The child cannot live safely with their birth family for a period of time and is placed with another caregiver.

Grammar notes

Transitivity
This sense is usually transitive and takes a child or children as its direct object.
Complements and objects
The direct object refers to the child receiving care. The verb may also appear without an expressed object when the context already makes the meaning clear.
Prepositions
No fixed preposition follows foster. Time expressions are common, as in foster a child for six months.
Form and position
Fostering is often used as a gerund when speaking generally about the activity.

Useful patterns

foster a child

Example: The family has enough space and support to foster a child.

foster children for a period of time

Example: They fostered three siblings for eight months.

be willing to foster

The object may be omitted when child care is already understood from the context.

Example: The agency is looking for experienced parents who are willing to foster.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: They fostered the child as their legal daughter.

Correct: They adopted the child as their legal daughter.

Fostering provides temporary care, while adoption creates a permanent legal parent-child relationship.

TOEFL use

Reading and listening

In social-science or community contexts, foster may refer to temporary care for a child rather than encouraging development.

Writing and speaking

Use this sense only when discussing officially arranged temporary family care. Use adopt for permanent legal parenthood.

Paraphrase tip

A suitable paraphrase is provide temporary family care for a child.

Meaning 3

Related to temporary family care

adjective /ˈfɔstər/ Standard and neutral; common in everyday, administrative, legal, and social-service language.

used to describe a child who is receiving temporary family care, an adult who provides that care, or the system and home connected with it

In simpler words: connected with temporary care for a child

Typical pattern: foster plus noun

Common contexts

foster carefoster parentsfoster familiesfoster homesfoster childrenchild welfare agencies

Natural examples

  1. Her foster parents encouraged her to stay in contact with her siblings.
  2. The city is trying to recruit more foster families for older children.
  3. He spent six months in a foster home before returning to his father.
  4. The report examines the educational experiences of children in foster care.
  5. A foster child may need time to feel secure in a new household.
  6. The agency provides counseling and financial assistance to foster caregivers.
  7. The siblings were placed with the same foster family.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The adjective marks a relationship based on temporary care rather than birth or legal adoption.

Usual implication: The arrangement is part of a child-welfare system or another formally organized care arrangement.

Grammar notes

Complements and objects
Foster appears before a noun and does not normally follow a linking verb in this meaning.
Prepositions
The fixed phrase in foster care is common when describing a child's situation.
Form and position
This is mainly an attributive adjective, as in foster parent, foster home, and foster care.

Useful patterns

foster parent or foster family

Example: Each foster family receives guidance from a trained social worker.

foster home

Example: The child remained in the same foster home throughout the school year.

in foster care

Example: Students in foster care may change schools more often than other students.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The child lives in a fostering home.

Correct: The child lives in a foster home.

The standard adjective before home is foster, not fostering.

Wrong: She is a foster of three children.

Correct: She is a foster parent to three children.

Foster is not normally used alone as a noun for the caregiver in standard learner English.

TOEFL use

Reading and listening

Use the nearby noun to identify the role: foster parent is the caregiver, foster child is the child, and foster care is the system or arrangement.

Writing and speaking

Use established combinations such as foster family, foster home, and foster care rather than creating new noun combinations.

Paraphrase tip

Depending on the noun, foster can be paraphrased as temporary caregiving or connected with temporary family placement.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

encourage

transitive verb · Encourage development

Difference: Encourage is broader and can also mean giving a person confidence or advice. Foster more strongly suggests supporting gradual development.

Example: The teacher encouraged students to ask questions.

promote

transitive verb · Encourage development

Difference: Promote often means actively supporting or advancing something, sometimes through a campaign or policy. Foster emphasizes creating conditions in which it can develop.

Example: The campaign promotes healthy eating among teenagers.

cultivate

transitive verb · Encourage development

Difference: Cultivate often suggests careful and deliberate development over time, especially of skills, attitudes, or relationships. Foster is slightly broader.

Example: The program helps students cultivate strong research habits.

nurture

transitive verb · Encourage development

Difference: Nurture often focuses on giving close care and attention to something that is already developing. Foster can refer more generally to creating supportive conditions.

Example: Good mentors nurture young researchers' confidence.

Direct or useful antonyms

inhibit

transitive verb · Encourage development

Opposition: Inhibit means to slow down, limit, or prevent development rather than encourage it.

Usage note: This is a strong opposite when the context involves growth, activity, or development.

Example: Fear of criticism can inhibit creative thinking.

discourage

transitive verb · Encourage development

Opposition: Discourage means to make an activity or development less likely rather than support it.

Usage note: Discourage is most natural when the context involves choices, behavior, interest, or participation.

Example: High fees may discourage students from applying.

Precision

Words learners may confuse

adopt

verb

Why it is confusing: Both words involve caring for a child who was not born into the family.

Key difference: To foster a child is to provide temporary care. To adopt a child is to become the child's permanent legal parent.

foster: The family fostered the child until she could return to her relatives.

adopt: The family later adopted another child and became her legal parents.

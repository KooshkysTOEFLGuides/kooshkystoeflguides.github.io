Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

Strongly enthusiastic and determined

adjective /ˈzɛləs/ More common in formal, political, historical, and religious contexts than in casual conversation.

showing great enthusiasm, energy, and determination in supporting a person, belief, goal, or activity

In simpler words: very enthusiastic and determined

Typical pattern: zealous + noun; zealous in + noun or -ing form; zealous about + noun or -ing form

Natural examples

  1. The organization’s zealous supporters worked for months to raise awareness of the issue.
  2. She was a zealous advocate for equal access to education.
  3. The reformers were zealous in their efforts to improve working conditions.
  4. His zealous commitment to environmental protection inspired many younger activists.
  5. The professor was zealous about encouraging students to question unsupported claims.
  6. A group of zealous volunteers helped rebuild the community center.
  7. The campaign attracted zealous followers who promoted its message across the country.
  8. Although the inspector was zealous in enforcing the rules, some employees felt that he was unnecessarily strict.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: Zealous suggests unusually strong enthusiasm combined with active effort and determination.

Usual implication: It can be positive when describing dedication, but it may also suggest excessive intensity or a lack of balance.

Compared with “enthusiastic”: Enthusiastic simply means excited and interested. Zealous suggests stronger commitment, greater effort, and sometimes excessive intensity.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use it before a noun, as in a zealous supporter, or after a form of be, as in She is zealous about the cause.
What can follow it
It commonly comes before nouns such as supporter, advocate, campaigner, follower, reformer, and commitment.
Common preposition
Use zealous about to name a subject someone strongly supports, or zealous in to describe an activity or effort. For example: He is zealous about conservation. She is zealous in defending human rights.
Position in a sentence
It often appears before a noun, but it can also appear after be, seem, or become.

Useful patterns

zealous + supporter, advocate, or campaigner

Use this pattern for a person who strongly and actively supports a cause, idea, or group.

Example: She became a zealous campaigner for improved public transportation.

be zealous about + noun or -ing form

Use this pattern to identify something that a person strongly supports or pursues.

Example: The director is zealous about improving workplace safety.

be zealous in + noun or -ing form

Use this pattern to describe an activity performed with great enthusiasm and determination.

Example: The volunteers were zealous in promoting the literacy program.

zealous commitment to + noun

Use this pattern to emphasize strong and energetic dedication to a goal or cause.

Example: Her zealous commitment to scientific accuracy earned the respect of her colleagues.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: She is zealous to environmental protection.

Correct: She is zealous about environmental protection.

Use zealous about when naming a subject or cause someone strongly supports.

Wrong: The volunteers worked zealous to promote the campaign.

Correct: The volunteers worked zealously to promote the campaign.

Use zealously to describe how an action is performed.

Wrong: He is a zealous for educational reform.

Correct: He is a zealous advocate for educational reform.

Zealous is an adjective, so it needs a noun such as advocate after it.

Wrong: The committee showed a zealous for improving safety.

Correct: The committee showed great zeal for improving safety.

Use the noun zeal after showed, not the adjective zealous.

Morphology

Word family

Only common and educationally useful family members are included.

uncountable noun

zeal

/ziːl/

great energy and enthusiasm in supporting or pursuing something

Relationship: Zeal is the noun from which zealous is formed.

Pattern: zeal for + noun; with zeal

Usage note: Zeal is normally used without a or an.

  1. The volunteers approached the project with remarkable zeal.
  2. Her zeal for educational reform influenced the entire organization.
  3. He defended the proposal with such zeal that the discussion became heated.

adverb

zealously

/ˈzɛləsli/

with great enthusiasm, energy, and determination

Relationship: Zealously is the adverb form of zealous.

Pattern: zealously + verb; verb + zealously

Usage note: Depending on the context, it can suggest either admirable dedication or excessive intensity.

  1. The lawyer zealously defended her client’s rights.
  2. The group zealously promoted the new environmental policy.
  3. Officials zealously enforced the regulations.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

enthusiastic

adjective · Strongly enthusiastic and determined

Shared meaning: Both words describe strong interest and positive energy.

Difference: Enthusiastic means excited and interested. Zealous suggests a stronger level of commitment and active effort.

Example: The students were enthusiastic about participating in the exchange program.

passionate

adjective · Strongly enthusiastic and determined

Shared meaning: Both words can describe strong commitment to something.

Difference: Passionate emphasizes strong emotion. Zealous emphasizes energetic dedication to a cause, belief, or goal and can sometimes suggest excessive intensity.

Example: She is passionate about preserving local cultural traditions.

fervent

adjective · Strongly enthusiastic and determined

Shared meaning: Both words describe intense support or belief.

Difference: Fervent emphasizes deep and sincere feeling, especially about beliefs or hopes. Zealous more strongly suggests active effort and determination.

Example: He remained a fervent believer in the value of peaceful negotiation.

ardent

adjective · Strongly enthusiastic and determined

Shared meaning: Both words describe very strong enthusiasm or support.

Difference: Ardent emphasizes strong and lasting emotion or admiration. Zealous often focuses more on energetic action in support of a cause.

Example: She was an ardent admirer of classical architecture.

devoted

adjective · Strongly enthusiastic and determined

Shared meaning: Both words can describe strong dedication.

Difference: Devoted emphasizes loyalty, affection, or long-term dedication. Zealous emphasizes energetic and sometimes intense support or action.

Example: He was a devoted teacher who spent decades helping disadvantaged students.

Direct or useful antonyms

apathetic

adjective · Strongly enthusiastic and determined

Opposition: Apathetic describes someone who has little interest, enthusiasm, or concern.

Usage note: This is a strong opposite because it suggests an almost complete lack of enthusiasm.

Example: Many voters remained apathetic and chose not to participate in the election.

indifferent

adjective · Strongly enthusiastic and determined

Opposition: Indifferent describes someone who does not care strongly about something.

Usage note: Indifferent emphasizes lack of concern rather than lack of energy.

Example: The public seemed largely indifferent to the proposed changes.

Precision

Words learners may confuse

jealous

adjective

Why it is confusing: The words have similar spelling and pronunciation, but their meanings are unrelated.

Key difference: Zealous means strongly enthusiastic and determined. Jealous means unhappy because someone has something you want, or worried that a relationship may be taken away.

zealous: She is a zealous supporter of wildlife conservation.

jealous: He felt jealous when his colleague received the award.

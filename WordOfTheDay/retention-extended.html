Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

Continuing to keep something

uncountable noun /rɪˈtɛnʃən/ Common in formal, academic, legal, administrative, and technical English.

the act of continuing to keep, possess, or control something instead of losing, removing, or giving it away

In simpler words: continuing to keep something

Typical pattern: retention of + noun

Natural examples

  1. The law requires the retention of financial records for at least five years.
  2. The policy allows employees to retain ownership of their original research, but the institution controls data retention.
  3. The preservation method improves the retention of nutrients during food processing.
  4. The agreement guarantees the retention of local control over the project.
  5. Careful drying techniques support the retention of the fruit's natural color and flavor.
  6. The museum developed strict rules for the retention and protection of donated objects.
  7. The new system was designed to improve data retention without increasing storage costs.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: Retention emphasizes that something remains available, present, owned, or controlled over time.

Usual implication: Without a particular action, rule, or system, the thing might otherwise be lost, removed, transferred, or reduced.

Compared with “preservation”: Preservation focuses on protecting something from damage or change. Retention focuses on continuing to keep or maintain possession of it.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Retention is normally used without a or an. Use retention of before the thing that is kept, as in the retention of records.
What can follow it
It is commonly followed by of and a noun such as data, records, ownership, control, nutrients, or information.
Common preposition
Use retention of something, not retention for something, when naming what is kept.
Noun use
Retention is normally uncountable, so do not usually say a retention or retentions.

Useful patterns

retention of + noun

Use this pattern to identify the thing that continues to be kept.

Example: The contract permits the retention of ownership by the original creator.

data or record retention

Use this pattern for rules or systems that determine how long information is stored.

Example: The organization revised its data retention policy.

improve or support retention

Use this pattern when a method helps something remain present or available.

Example: Lower cooking temperatures may improve nutrient retention.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The company requires the retention for all customer records.

Correct: The company requires the retention of all customer records.

Use retention of to name what is kept.

Wrong: The retention of the data are required by law.

Correct: The retention of the data is required by law.

Retention is singular and uncountable, so use is.

Wrong: The policy supports a retention of local ownership.

Correct: The policy supports the retention of local ownership.

Retention is normally uncountable. In this structure, the retention of is natural.

Meaning 2

Ability to remember

uncountable noun /rɪˈtɛnʃən/ Common in education, psychology, language learning, and cognitive science.

the ability to remember information or skills after learning them

In simpler words: ability to remember what you learned

Typical pattern: retention of + information or skill; memory retention; long-term retention

Natural examples

  1. Regular review can improve the long-term retention of new vocabulary.
  2. The researchers measured information retention one week after the lecture.
  3. Students showed better retention when they connected new ideas with previous knowledge.
  4. Lack of sleep can reduce memory retention and concentration.
  5. The experiment compared the retention of facts learned through reading and listening.
  6. Frequent practice supports the retention of complex skills.
  7. The teacher used short quizzes to check students' retention of the material.
  8. Active participation often leads to stronger retention than passive listening.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: This sense focuses on whether learned information or skills remain in memory over time.

Usual implication: Retention is usually measured after some time has passed, not immediately after learning.

Compared with “recall”: Retention is the continued storage of information in memory. Recall is the act of bringing that information back to mind at a particular moment.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Use retention alone when the subject is clear, or use retention of before the information or skill being remembered.
What can follow it
It often appears with memory, information, knowledge, vocabulary, facts, skills, learning, and long-term.
Common preposition
Say retention of vocabulary or retention of information.
Noun use
Retention is uncountable in this meaning. Say good retention or poor retention, not a retention.

Useful patterns

retention of + information or skill

Use this pattern to identify what remains in memory.

Example: The study examined the retention of scientific vocabulary.

improve or increase retention

Use this pattern for a method that helps learners remember more.

Example: Spaced review can significantly improve retention.

long-term retention

Use this expression for remembering information over an extended period.

Example: Meaningful examples support long-term retention.

memory retention

Use this expression when discussing the ability to store learned information.

Example: Stress can have a negative effect on memory retention.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: Daily practice improves the retention for new words.

Correct: Daily practice improves the retention of new words.

Use retention of to name what learners remember.

Wrong: The students had a strong retention of the lecture.

Correct: The students showed strong retention of the lecture content.

Retention is normally uncountable, and lecture content is more precise than lecture here.

Wrong: The test measured how much information students retentioned.

Correct: The test measured how much information students retained.

Retention is a noun. The related verb is retain.

Meaning 3

Keeping people in an organization

uncountable noun /rɪˈtɛnʃən/ Very common in business, education, health care, human resources, and public policy.

the ability of an organization, business, or program to keep its employees, customers, members, or students

In simpler words: success in keeping employees, customers, or students

Typical pattern: employee, staff, customer, or student retention; retention of + people

Natural examples

  1. Flexible working conditions have improved employee retention.
  2. The university introduced new support services to increase student retention.
  3. High customer retention can reduce a company's marketing costs.
  4. Low salaries were identified as a major cause of poor staff retention.
  5. Mentoring programs may improve teacher retention in rural schools.
  6. The company studies customer satisfaction because it strongly affects retention.
  7. A sense of belonging is an important factor in student retention.
  8. The hospital offered additional training to support the retention of experienced nurses.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: This sense measures whether people continue working for, buying from, belonging to, or studying at an organization.

Usual implication: High retention is usually viewed positively because replacing employees, customers, or students can be difficult and expensive.

Compared with “recruitment”: Recruitment means attracting new people to an organization. Retention means keeping the people who are already there.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Place the type of person before retention, as in employee retention, or use retention of followed by a group.
What can follow it
It commonly appears after employee, staff, teacher, customer, student, member, and patient.
Common preposition
Use retention of employees when the group comes after the word.
Noun use
Retention is uncountable. Say improve retention, not improve a retention.

Useful patterns

employee, staff, or teacher retention

Use this pattern for an organization's ability to keep workers.

Example: Better promotion opportunities may improve employee retention.

customer retention

Use this expression for a business's ability to keep existing customers.

Example: Reliable service is essential for customer retention.

student retention

Use this expression for keeping students enrolled in a school or program.

Example: Academic advising can improve student retention.

retention rate

Use this expression for the percentage of people who remain over a particular period.

Example: The company reported a staff retention rate of ninety percent.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The company wants to improve the retention of its customers in the company.

Correct: The company wants to improve customer retention.

Customer retention is the shorter and more natural expression.

Wrong: Higher salaries can increase employees retention.

Correct: Higher salaries can increase employee retention.

Use the singular form employee before another noun.

Wrong: The school has a good students retention rate.

Correct: The school has a good student retention rate.

Use student, not students, before retention rate.

Meaning 4

Holding a substance or quality

uncountable noun /rɪˈtɛnʃən/ Common in environmental science, agriculture, medicine, engineering, and materials science.

the ability or process of holding a substance, especially water, heat, or fluid, instead of releasing it

In simpler words: ability to hold something instead of releasing it

Typical pattern: water, heat, moisture, fluid, or color retention

Natural examples

  1. Adding organic matter to soil can improve water retention.
  2. Thick walls increase heat retention during cold weather.
  3. Excessive salt consumption may contribute to fluid retention in the body.
  4. The material was selected for its excellent moisture retention.
  5. Forests improve water retention and reduce the risk of soil erosion.
  6. The container's design supports heat retention for several hours.
  7. Poor drainage can lead to excessive water retention in agricultural fields.
  8. Some fabrics provide better color retention after repeated washing.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: This sense describes how well a body, material, soil, or object keeps a substance or quality inside it.

Usual implication: Retention may be useful, as with soil moisture or building heat, or harmful, as with excessive fluid retention in the body.

Compared with “absorption”: Absorption is the process of taking a substance in. Retention is the ability to continue holding it after it has been taken in.

Grammar notes

How to use it
Place the substance before retention, as in water retention, or use retention of followed by the substance.
What can follow it
It commonly appears with water, moisture, heat, fluid, color, and nutrients.
Common preposition
Use retention of heat when the substance follows the noun.
Noun use
Retention is uncountable in this meaning.

Useful patterns

water or moisture retention

Use this expression for the ability of soil, plants, or materials to hold water.

Example: Clay soils generally have greater water retention than sandy soils.

heat retention

Use this expression for the ability of a material or structure to keep heat.

Example: Insulation improves heat retention inside the building.

fluid retention

Use this expression when the body holds more fluid than normal.

Example: The medication may cause temporary fluid retention.

retention of + substance or quality

Use this pattern when the substance comes after the noun.

Example: The coating improves the retention of color over time.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: This soil has a high retention of water capacity.

Correct: This soil has a high water-retention capacity.

Use water-retention before capacity, or say this soil has a high capacity for water retention.

Wrong: The walls improve the heat's retention.

Correct: The walls improve heat retention.

Heat retention is the natural expression.

Wrong: Too much salt can cause the body to retention fluid.

Correct: Too much salt can cause the body to retain fluid.

Retention is a noun. Use the verb retain after to.

Morphology

Word family

Only common and educationally useful family members are included.

transitive verb

retain

/rɪˈteɪn/

to continue to have, keep, remember, or hold something

Relationship: Retain is the verb from which retention is formed.

Pattern: retain + object

Usage note: Retain normally needs a person, thing, quality, substance, or piece of information after it.

  1. Students retain information more effectively when they review it regularly.
  2. The soil retains enough moisture to support plant growth.
  3. The company introduced new benefits to retain experienced employees.

adjective

retentive

/rɪˈtɛntɪv/

able to hold or remember something well

Relationship: Retentive describes a person, memory, material, or soil that retains something effectively.

Pattern: a retentive memory; retentive soil

Usage note: This adjective is less common than retain and retention and is mainly used with memory, soil, and materials.

  1. She has a highly retentive memory for names and dates.
  2. The soil's retentive properties help plants survive dry periods.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

preservation

uncountable noun · Continuing to keep something

Shared meaning: Both words can involve keeping something over time.

Difference: Preservation emphasizes protecting something from damage, decay, or change. Retention emphasizes continuing to keep or maintain possession of it.

Example: The organization supports the preservation of historic buildings.

memory

countable and uncountable noun · Ability to remember

Shared meaning: Both words relate to storing and remembering information.

Difference: Memory is the general ability or process of remembering. Retention specifically concerns how well information remains available after learning.

Example: Her memory for names is unusually strong.

continuation

countable and uncountable noun · Keeping people in an organization

Shared meaning: Both words involve something remaining in place over time.

Difference: Continuation broadly means that something keeps happening. Retention specifically refers to keeping people within a company, program, or institution.

Example: The continuation of the program depends on future funding.

storage

uncountable noun · Holding a substance or quality

Shared meaning: Both words involve keeping something rather than releasing or losing it.

Difference: Storage emphasizes placing and keeping something for future use. Retention emphasizes the ability to prevent a substance or quality from escaping or being lost.

Example: The facility provides secure storage for medical supplies.

Direct or useful antonyms

loss

countable and uncountable noun · all senses

Opposition: Loss describes no longer having, keeping, remembering, or holding something.

Usage note: This is the broadest opposite and can apply to information, employees, heat, nutrients, or memory.

Example: Heat loss through the windows increased energy costs.

turnover

uncountable noun · Keeping people in an organization

Opposition: Turnover refers to employees or members leaving and being replaced, while retention refers to keeping them.

Usage note: This contrast is especially common in employment contexts.

Example: Poor management contributed to high staff turnover.

forgetting

uncountable noun · Ability to remember

Opposition: Forgetting is the process of losing information from memory, while retention is the continued ability to remember it.

Usage note: This is the most direct opposite in learning and memory contexts.

Example: Without review, forgetting may occur rapidly.

release

uncountable noun · Holding a substance or quality

Opposition: Release means allowing a substance such as heat, water, or fluid to leave rather than continuing to hold it.

Usage note: This contrast applies mainly to physical substances and energy.

Example: The slow release of heat kept the room warm overnight.

Precision

Words learners may confuse

detention

noun

Why it is confusing: The words have similar spelling and pronunciation but very different meanings.

Key difference: Retention means continuing to keep, remember, or hold something. Detention means being kept in a place, often as punishment or by legal authority.

retention: Regular review improves vocabulary retention.

detention: The student received detention for repeatedly arriving late.

Quick orientation

Usage overview

Core idea

The most common sense describes someone or something with a natural tendency or higher risk of a particular result.

Register

The tendency sense is common in neutral and formal English, especially in academic, medical, scientific, and news contexts. The body-position sense is more formal and technical.

Where it appears

Learners often encounter prone in discussions of health, environmental risks, human behavior, technical problems, and physical position.

TOEFL value

Prone is useful for describing risk, tendency, and vulnerability in academic reading, lectures, and cause-and-effect writing.

Dictionary guide

Meanings and usage

The core definition and examples are always included. Additional notes appear only when they are accurate and useful.

Meaning 1

Likely or susceptible

adjective /proʊn/ Neutral to formal; especially common in academic, medical, scientific, and technical contexts.

likely to experience something harmful or unpleasant, or likely to behave in a particular way

In simpler words: likely to have, suffer from, or do something

Typical pattern: be prone to plus noun or gerund

Common contexts

health problemsnatural disasterstechnical failureshuman behaviorerrorsinjuriesenvironmental risks

Natural examples

  1. Coastal areas are prone to flooding during severe storms.
  2. Young children are more prone to dehydration than healthy adults.
  3. People who are prone to worrying may find uncertainty especially stressful.
  4. The older version of the software was prone to unexpected crashes.
  5. Plants grown in wet soil are more prone to fungal diseases.
  6. He is prone to making quick decisions without considering the consequences.
  7. Mountain roads are particularly prone to landslides after heavy rainfall.
  8. Some laboratory measurements are prone to error when the equipment is not properly calibrated.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: Prone suggests that something happens more easily or more often than usual because of a person's nature, an object's design, or existing conditions.

Usual implication: The result is usually negative, undesirable, or risky.

Compared with “likely”: Likely can describe a prediction based on current evidence. Prone usually describes a continuing tendency, weakness, or vulnerability.

Grammar notes

Complements and objects
Prone is normally followed by to and then a noun, pronoun, or gerund. A to-infinitive is also possible, especially with repeated behavior, but the gerund pattern is often more natural for learners.
Prepositions
Use prone to, not prone for or prone with.
Form and position
This sense usually appears after a linking verb, as in is prone to error. It also appears in compound adjectives before nouns, such as accident-prone workers and flood-prone regions.

Useful patterns

be prone to plus noun

Use this to describe a higher risk of a condition, event, or problem.

Example: Older buildings may be prone to structural damage during earthquakes.

be prone to plus gerund

Use this to describe a repeated tendency to behave in a certain way.

Example: He is prone to exaggerating minor problems.

more or less prone to

Use comparative forms to compare levels of risk or tendency.

Example: Certain crops are more prone to disease in humid climates.

noun plus prone

Prone can form a compound adjective identifying the type of problem that commonly occurs.

Example: The engineers replaced several error-prone procedures with automated checks.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: This region is prone for earthquakes.

Correct: This region is prone to earthquakes.

The adjective prone is followed by the preposition to.

Wrong: She is prone to make careless mistakes.

Correct: She is prone to making careless mistakes.

A gerund is generally the most natural form after prone to when describing repeated behavior.

Wrong: The machine is prone with overheating.

Correct: The machine is prone to overheating.

Use prone to followed by a noun or gerund.

Wrong: Heavy rain made the road prone to be flooded.

Correct: Heavy rain made the road prone to flooding.

A noun or gerund is more natural than a passive infinitive after prone to.

TOEFL use

Reading and listening

Prone to often signals an increased risk or a repeated tendency. The cause of that vulnerability may appear before or after the phrase.

Writing and speaking

Use prone to with specific nouns such as flooding, disease, error, damage, or conflict. It is especially useful when comparing risks.

Paraphrase tip

Depending on context, prone to can mean likely to experience, susceptible to, vulnerable to, or having a tendency to.

Meaning 2

Lying face downward

adjective /proʊn/ Formal and technical; common in medical, anatomical, military, and descriptive contexts.

lying flat with the front of the body facing downward

In simpler words: lying face down

Typical pattern: lie prone or be in a prone position

Common contexts

medicineanatomyphysical exerciseemergency responsemilitary movementdescriptions of accidents

Natural examples

  1. The patient was placed in a prone position during the examination.
  2. The archaeologist lay prone on the ground to examine the markings closely.
  3. The injured hiker was found lying prone beside the trail.
  4. The exercise begins with the participant in a prone position.
  5. The photographer remained prone in the grass while waiting for the bird to appear.
  6. Rescuers carefully turned the prone man onto his back.
  7. The soldiers moved into a prone position to remain hidden.
  8. Researchers recorded the participants' breathing while they were lying prone.

Nuance and implication

Central nuance: The chest and front of the body face the surface beneath the person.

Usual implication: The word precisely identifies body orientation and does not by itself explain why the person is in that position.

Compared with “supine”: Prone means lying face downward, while supine means lying on the back with the face upward.

Grammar notes

Complements and objects
Prone commonly follows lie, remain, be found, or be placed. It also appears before position as an attributive adjective.
Prepositions
Common patterns include prone on the ground and in a prone position.
Form and position
It is often used after a verb in the phrase lie prone or before the noun position. The phrase lying prone is especially common.

Useful patterns

lie prone

Example: The scientist lay prone beside the nest to observe the birds without disturbing them.

be found lying prone

Example: The missing climber was found lying prone near the base of the hill.

in a prone position

This is the standard formal expression for the face-down body position.

Example: The procedure requires the patient to remain in a prone position.

place someone in a prone position

Example: The medical team placed the patient in a prone position to improve breathing.

Common wrong usage

Wrong: The patient was lying prone on his back.

Correct: The patient was lying supine on his back.

Prone means face down. Supine means lying on the back.

Wrong: She was in prone position.

Correct: She was in a prone position.

Position is a singular countable noun here, so it needs the article a.

Wrong: He laid prone on the floor.

Correct: He lay prone on the floor.

The past tense of the intransitive verb lie is lay. Laid is the past tense of lay, which normally takes an object.

TOEFL use

Reading and listening

In medical or scientific contexts, prone identifies a precise physical position and contrasts with supine.

Writing and speaking

For general communication, face down is often simpler. Use prone when a formal or technical description is appropriate.

Paraphrase tip

Prone in this sense can usually be paraphrased as lying face downward or in a face-down position.

Meaning relations

Synonyms and antonyms

Only clear and useful relationships are included.

Close synonyms

susceptible

adjective · Likely or susceptible

Shared meaning: Both words can describe a person or thing that is easily affected by a problem.

Difference: Susceptible often emphasizes being easily affected by an outside influence, especially disease, damage, or persuasion. Prone can also describe a repeated personal behavior.

Example: Young plants are highly susceptible to frost.

vulnerable

adjective · Likely or susceptible

Shared meaning: Both can indicate an increased risk of harm.

Difference: Vulnerable emphasizes weakness or lack of protection. Prone emphasizes a tendency or higher probability.

Example: Small islands are particularly vulnerable to rising sea levels.

inclined

adjective · Likely or susceptible

Shared meaning: Both can describe a tendency to behave in a particular way.

Difference: Inclined is often neutral and may describe a preference or willingness. Prone usually suggests a repeated and often undesirable tendency.

Example: She is inclined to avoid unnecessary risks.

likely

adjective · Likely or susceptible

Shared meaning: Both can indicate that something has a high chance of happening.

Difference: Likely often describes a prediction about one situation. Prone usually describes a continuing tendency or vulnerability.

Example: The roads are likely to become icy overnight.

face down

adverbial phrase · Lying face downward

Shared meaning: Both describe the same basic body orientation.

Difference: Face down is more common in everyday English, while prone is more formal and technical.

Example: The swimmer floated face down for several seconds.

Direct or useful antonyms

resistant

adjective · Likely or susceptible

Opposition: Resistant describes something that is not easily affected by a particular problem or force.

Usage note: It is an opposite only when prone describes susceptibility to a specific influence.

Example: This variety of wheat is resistant to several common diseases.

supine

adjective · Lying face downward

Opposition: Supine means lying on the back with the face upward.

Usage note: This is a formal anatomical opposite of prone.

Example: The patient remained supine throughout the procedure.

Precision

Words learners may confuse

likely

adjective

Why it is confusing: Both words can indicate that something has a high chance of happening.

Key difference: Likely often predicts a particular future event, while prone usually describes a continuing tendency or vulnerability.

prone: This type of engine is prone to overheating.

likely: The engine is likely to overheat during today's test.

supine

adjective

Why it is confusing: Both are formal terms used to describe body position.

Key difference: Prone means lying face downward, while supine means lying on the back with the face upward.

prone: The patient was placed in a prone position.

supine: The patient was placed in a supine position.

TOEFL Word of the Day

imply

/ɪmˈplaɪ/

Imply means to suggest something without saying it directly, or to make something seem naturally or logically true.

June 29th 2026 @KooshkyToefl

01 Meanings and Usage

1
verb · transitive
/ɪmˈplaɪ/

To suggest an idea, feeling, or message without saying it directly.

Core idea: The meaning is hidden or indirect, but the listener or reader can understand it.

Examples

  • His silence implied that he did not agree with the decision.
  • The article implies that poor planning caused the problem.
  • Her smile seemed to imply that she already knew the answer.
  • The professor did not say the exam would be difficult, but he implied it.
  • The results imply that students learn better when they receive regular feedback.

Nuance and implication

Imply is about the speaker, writer, evidence, or situation. It means the message is suggested, not directly stated. In TOEFL reading, writers often imply ideas through evidence, tone, contrast, or word choice.

Grammar notes

  • imply + that-clause: The speaker implied that the plan had failed.
  • imply + noun: The data implies a serious problem.
  • be implied by: This conclusion is implied by the evidence.
  • Do not use imply to before a person.

Common wrong usage

✗ The teacher implied me that I should study more.

✓ The teacher implied that I should study more.

Imply does not take a person as the direct object. Use a that-clause for the suggested message.

2
verb · transitive
/ɪmˈplaɪ/

To make something necessary, expected, or naturally connected with something else.

Core idea: One thing brings another thing with it as a natural result.

Examples

  • A leadership role implies responsibility, patience, and clear communication.
  • Buying a house often implies a long-term financial commitment.
  • Scientific research implies careful observation and honest reporting.
  • Freedom of speech does not imply freedom from criticism.
  • Accepting the scholarship implies that the student agrees to the university’s conditions.

Nuance and implication

In this meaning, imply is not about hidden speech. It means one idea includes or brings another idea. It is common in academic writing when explaining consequences, responsibilities, conditions, or assumptions.

Grammar notes

  • subject + imply + noun: Success implies effort.
  • subject + imply + that-clause: The rule implies that all students must register.
  • This use is more formal and academic than everyday alternatives like mean or involve.

Common wrong usage

✗ This job implies to work on weekends.

✓ This job implies working on weekends.

After imply, use a noun, gerund, or that-clause. Do not use imply to do.

3
verb · transitive
/ɪmˈplaɪ/

To mean something as a logical result, especially in reasoning or explanation.

Core idea: If one statement is true, another statement follows from it.

Examples

  • If the store is closed on Mondays, that implies we cannot go there today.
  • The phrase “limited time” implies that the offer will end soon.
  • The evidence does not necessarily imply that the theory is wrong.
  • If all participants passed the test, it implies that Ali passed too.
  • The author’s argument implies that education affects economic development.

Nuance and implication

This meaning is especially useful for TOEFL reading and academic discussion. It often appears with words like therefore, evidence, argument, conclusion, and suggest.

Grammar notes

  • This implies that... is very common in academic writing.
  • Not necessarily imply means the conclusion may be possible, but it is not certain.
  • imply is often stronger and more logical than hint.

Common wrong usage

✗ The evidence implies to the conclusion.

✓ The evidence implies the conclusion.

✓ The evidence implies that the conclusion may be correct.

Use imply + noun or imply that + clause, not imply to.

02 Word Family

implication

Part of speech: noun

Definition: a possible result, effect, or hidden meaning of something.

  • The discovery has important implications for medical research.
  • The implication of his comment was that the team had failed.

Usage note: Common in academic English: implications for/of.

implicit

Part of speech: adjective

Definition: suggested or understood without being said directly.

  • There was an implicit criticism in her question.
  • The article contains an implicit warning about climate change.

Usage note: Opposite of explicit.

implicitly

Part of speech: adverb

Definition: in a way that is suggested but not directly stated.

  • The author implicitly criticizes the government’s policy.
  • The rule implicitly allows students to work in groups.

implied

Part of speech: adjective

Definition: suggested without being directly stated.

  • The implied meaning of the sentence is negative.
  • The contract includes several implied responsibilities.

Usage note: Although implied is also a past form, it is useful as an adjective.

03 Common Collocations

Collocation Meaning Example
strongly imply to suggest something very clearly without saying it directly The study strongly implies that exercise improves memory.
seem to imply to appear to suggest something His answer seemed to imply that he had doubts about the plan.
evidence implies facts suggest a conclusion The evidence implies that the species migrated earlier than expected.
data implies numbers or research results suggest something The data implies a relationship between sleep quality and test performance.
does not necessarily imply does not prove or automatically mean A high income does not necessarily imply happiness.
imply responsibility to bring responsibility as a natural result Being a team leader implies responsibility for other people’s work.
imply a connection to suggest that two things are related The findings imply a connection between diet and concentration.
imply criticism to indirectly show criticism Her question implied criticism of the company’s decision.

04 Synonyms and Antonyms

Meaning Synonyms Nuance Notes Antonyms
to suggest something indirectly suggest, hint, indicate, insinuate Suggest is general and common. Hint is lighter and less direct. Indicate is often used with evidence. Insinuate can sound negative. state, say directly, express explicitly, declare
to make something necessary or naturally connected involve, require, entail, mean Involve is common and practical. Entail is formal and means “include as a necessary result.” Mean is simpler and more general. exclude, rule out, prevent, contradict
to mean something as a logical result show, indicate, lead to the conclusion, suggest Show is stronger when proof is clear. Suggest is weaker. Lead to the conclusion is useful in academic explanations. disprove, contradict, rule out, fail to show

05 Etymology

Imply comes from Latin roots meaning “to fold in” or “to involve.” This history helps explain the modern meaning: an idea is not openly stated, but it is “included inside” the message, situation, or evidence.

06 Exercises

1. Fill in the blank with the correct word-family member.

The author never says the policy failed, but the criticism is clearly __________.

Options: imply · implication · implicit · implicitly

2. Choose the correct meaning in context.

“A higher position in the company implies greater responsibility.”

What does implies mean here?

A) says directly · B) makes naturally connected · C) hides completely · D) asks politely

3. Correct the wrong usage.

The professor implied us that the topic would be on the exam.

4. Natural or unnatural?

“The evidence does not necessarily imply that the theory is correct.”

5. Choose the best collocation.

The survey results __________ a connection between screen time and sleep problems.

A) strongly imply · B) strongly say · C) strongly speak · D) strongly tell

6. Short production task

Write one academic sentence using imply about education, technology, health, or the environment.

07 Answer Key

  1. implicit — It describes criticism that is suggested but not directly stated.
  2. B — The position naturally brings greater responsibility.
  3. The professor implied that the topic would be on the exam. Do not use a person directly after imply.
  4. Natural. The phrase does not necessarily imply is common in academic English.
  5. A — Strongly imply is a natural collocation.
  6. Sample answer: The study implies that regular sleep can improve students’ academic performance.
